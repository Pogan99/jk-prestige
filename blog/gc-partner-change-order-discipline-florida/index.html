Change Order Discipline: What Disciplined Trade Subs Do Differently on Florida Projects

June 7, 2026 · 6 min read · JK Prestige Constructor Corp

Change order theater is one of the most reliable ways for a trade sub to get removed from a GC's bid list. Not the change order itself — changes are part of construction. The theater. The two-week delay on pricing. The vague scope language designed to leave room for a second bite. The verbal agreement that becomes a dispute three months later when the final invoice doesn't match anyone's notes.

At JK Prestige Constructor, we've worked with subs who handle change orders like professionals and subs who treat every change as an opportunity to recover margin they didn't get in the base contract. The difference between those two types shows up fast, and it determines who we call first on the next job.

Here's what disciplined trade subs do differently.

Price Changes Within 48 Hours

The schedule doesn't stop for change order negotiations. When a field condition changes — the architect adds a wall, the owner revises the finish, the structural engineer adds a beam — the work either stops (costing the project money) or proceeds on a directive basis (creating liability exposure).

The fastest path through a change is a priced proposal from the sub within 48 hours of the direction being issued. Not a placeholder. A real number, with breakdown.

Subs who consistently turn pricing in under 48 hours get trust. GCs start to route changes to them as a matter of habit. The sub who takes two weeks to price a $6,000 change doesn't get asked for the next one — the GC finds someone who can respond.

The 48-hour window is real. We do not expect subs to guess on complex structural changes that require engineering input. When a change requires engineering, the sub should say so within 24 hours and give a timeline. Silence and delay are the problems — not complexity.

Break Out Labor, Material, and Equipment Separately

A change order priced as a lump sum with no breakdown tells the GC nothing useful and gets negotiated harder than a fully documented proposal.

The format we want from trade subs:

  • Labor: hours by trade, fully burdened rate (including burden, fringe, supervision)
  • Material: unit prices with quantities, or quoted material cost with supplier name
  • Equipment: daily or weekly rates, mobilization costs if applicable
  • Markup: stated as a percentage, applied to direct costs only (not to markup on markup)
  • Schedule impact: stated in calendar days, with a brief explanation

The schedule impact line is frequently left out. Don't leave it out. If the change adds five days to your critical path work, that's information the GC needs to communicate to the owner. If the change has no schedule impact, write that — it prevents a dispute later.

Use Scope Language That Can't Be Misread

Change order scope language is where disputes originate. "Install drywall per revised plan" means nothing six months later when there's a dispute about whether the corridor revision was included in that change or in the base contract.

Disciplined subs write scope language that references:

  • The specific drawing revision or ASI (Architect's Supplemental Instruction) number that triggered the change
  • The specific room, grid line, or zone affected
  • What is included and what is explicitly excluded
  • Whether the change supersedes any previously executed work in that area

"Install 5/8-inch Type X drywall per ASI-7 (Revision 2), Grid Lines C through F, Level 2 corridor, including one layer ceiling, two layers to deck at rated assembly locations. Excludes taping and finishing (see separate proposal for finishes sub)." That's a scope statement that holds up.

"Drywall per revision" does not hold up.

Get the Authorization in Writing Before the Work Begins

The most common change order dispute pattern in Florida construction: the GC's superintendent tells the sub's foreman to proceed. The foreman proceeds. The change doesn't get formally authorized. Billing time comes and the sub can't collect because there's no signed change order in the file.

Florida's construction lien law does not protect you from an unauthorized change. Your lien rights attach to the contract value. Work performed outside of a signed contract or a signed change order is work you may not be able to collect on.

The professional approach is to get an authorization before the work starts. Not necessarily a fully executed change order with the owner's signature — those take time on large jobs. But a written directive from the GC authorizing you to proceed, with the understood price, gets you protected.

At JK Prestige, we use a Directive to Proceed for field changes where we've agreed on direction but the formal change order paperwork hasn't cleared the owner's desk yet. The DTP states the scope, the understood cost, and the authorization date. The formal change order follows and references the DTP.

If a GC tells you to proceed verbally and won't give you a DTP, document it yourself: send a same-day email to the GC confirming the direction, the scope, and the understood pricing. Write "please let me know if any of this is inaccurate." If they don't respond, your email is your documentation.

Track Changes Against the Original Scope

Scope creep is real, and it runs in both directions. Sometimes a change adds scope you weren't responsible for under the original contract. Sometimes a change substitutes work you already priced and removes scope you expected to perform.

Disciplined subs track every change against their original scope of work. When the net of all changes causes their contract value to shift materially — say, more than 10% — they bring it up proactively before billing.

The sub who tracks this and communicates it is managing the project like a partner. The sub who tracks it silently and then presents a surprise overage at the end of the job is managing the project like someone who knows they're about to create a dispute.

Don't Use Change Orders to Recover Bid Margin

This is the category that gets subs removed from bid lists permanently.

Every GC who has been doing this for more than five years has a mental list of subs who bid low and then price changes aggressively to recover the margin they left out of the base bid. It's a detectable pattern: the base bid is 12% below market, and the first two changes come in 35% above market rate.

The pattern does not go unnoticed. It poisons the relationship, it creates disputes, and it gets you removed from the next invitation.

Bid what the work costs. Price changes what the work costs. If you've made a genuine error in the base bid, bring it up at the pre-contract meeting — not through change order markups.
