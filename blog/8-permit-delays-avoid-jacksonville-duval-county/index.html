8 Permit Delays to Avoid in Jacksonville and Duval County

May 4, 2026 · 7 min read · JK Prestige Constructor Corp

The City of Jacksonville's building permit process runs through the Duval County CityView portal, and each correction cycle from the plan review team adds a minimum of two to four weeks to your project timeline. That's not a bureaucratic inconvenience — it's a real schedule impact. A project expecting to break ground in April can easily slide to June if a submittal package has two correction cycles, each requiring document revision and resubmittal.

The good news is that most permit delays are predictable and preventable. After 500-plus projects in the Jacksonville market, JK Prestige Construction has seen the same eight issues come up repeatedly. Getting ahead of them before submittal is the most reliable way to hold your construction start date.

1. Incomplete Drawing Sets at Submittal

The most common cause of a correction notice is a drawing set that doesn't meet the completeness requirements for plan review. Duval County requires specific sheets depending on project type: site plan, floor plan, foundation plan, framing plan, electrical, plumbing, mechanical, and energy compliance documentation are all typically required for new construction. Missing a sheet — even one that seems minor — triggers a correction that stops the review clock.

The fix is a pre-submittal checklist cross-referenced against the current Duval County building department requirements, which are published on the Jacksonville.gov development services site and updated periodically. GCs who submit frequently maintain their own checklists and update them after each correction notice to catch recurrent gaps. Submitting a complete set on the first pass is the most reliable way to reduce permit lead time.

2. Missing Energy Code Calculations for New Construction

Florida follows the Florida Energy Efficiency Code for Building Construction, which is built on ASHRAE 90.1 for commercial projects and the IECC for residential. New construction submittals require energy compliance documentation — typically a COMcheck report for commercial or a REScheck report for residential — demonstrating that the building envelope, mechanical systems, and lighting meet code requirements.

Submitting drawings without this documentation is a guaranteed correction. The calculations must be complete, signed, and consistent with the drawings — a REScheck showing R-19 wall insulation when the drawings specify R-13 will also generate a correction. In Jacksonville's humid subtropical climate, envelope performance requirements are specific to Climate Zone 2A, and the calculations need to reflect that zone correctly.

3. Structural Engineering Not on FBPE-Licensed Engineer's Letterhead

Florida Building Code requires that structural drawings be signed and sealed by an engineer licensed by the Florida Board of Professional Engineers (FBPE). This is not optional and it's not satisfied by an out-of-state engineer's seal — the engineer of record for structural systems must hold a valid Florida PE license.

In practice, this issue comes up when a design team uses an out-of-state structural engineer who is familiar with the project but not licensed in Florida, or when structural calculations are provided by the architect's office without a separate structural engineer of record. Duval County plan review will flag this immediately. For projects in Jacksonville, confirm that your structural engineer of record has a current FBPE license number before the drawings are finalized.

4. Expired Survey

Most commercial projects and many residential additions in Duval County require a current survey — typically within 12 months of the submittal date. An expired survey triggers a correction because setback compliance, flood zone determination, and site coverage calculations depend on current property data.

This is a common issue on projects where the design phase ran long: a survey commissioned at the start of design is 14 months old by the time the drawings are ready to submit. Order a field update from the surveyor of record rather than a full resurvey — it costs less and takes less time, but it resets the survey date for submittal purposes. Flag the survey date early in the pre-submittal checklist so it doesn't become a last-minute surprise.

5. Contractor License Not Active at Time of Submittal

In Florida, a building permit can only be pulled by a licensed contractor. The contractor's license must be active — not expired, not on probation — at the time of submittal. Duval County verifies license status through the Florida Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR) database.

This is straightforward to prevent: confirm that the general contractor's Florida Certified General Contractor (CGC) or Certified Building Contractor (CBC) license is current before submitting. It's also worth confirming any required specialty licenses — roofing, plumbing, mechanical, electrical — are active for the relevant trades on the project. JK Prestige maintains current licensure on all required trade categories and verifies renewal dates on a scheduled basis.

6. Outstanding Violations on the Property from a Previous Permit

If a property has an open code violation or an expired permit with outstanding inspections, Duval County building services may hold new permit issuance until the prior issue is resolved. This is a common problem on commercial property acquisitions: the new owner buys the property, starts design, and discovers at submittal that a previous tenant pulled a permit for a build-out and never closed it out.

The fix is a lien and permit history search on the property before design begins — not at the time of submittal. A title company or permit expediter can pull the property's permit history from the CityView system. Resolving an open violation or expired permit can take four to eight weeks depending on what's required, so finding it early matters.

7. Incomplete Zoning Compliance Documentation

Building permits require zoning compliance as a prerequisite. For commercial projects, this means confirming that the proposed use is permitted in the applicable zoning district and that the site plan meets dimensional standards: setbacks, lot coverage, height, parking count, and impervious surface calculations.

Incomplete or missing zoning documentation — such as a parking analysis that doesn't account for the proposed use's code-required ratio — triggers a correction from the zoning review section, which is separate from the building review section. Both must clear before the permit issues. In Jacksonville's urban core areas, including Downtown Development Review Authority (DDRA) districts, additional zoning approvals may be required before building submittal.

8. Missing DRC Approval for Projects in Certain Overlay Districts

Jacksonville has several overlay districts — including historic preservation zones, the Urban Development Area, and Planned Unit Development (PUD) areas — where a project may require Development Review Committee (DRC) approval before or concurrent with building permit submittal. The DRC process involves a separate application, a public meeting cycle, and a board approval that runs on its own timeline.

Projects in Riverside/Avondale, Springfield, or other locally designated historic districts also require the Historic Preservation Commission (HPC) review for certain work types. Missing this upstream approval creates a hard stop at permit submittal. Confirm DRC and HPC applicability in the pre-design phase, not after drawings are complete, to avoid building your schedule around a permit that can't issue until a separate board acts.
