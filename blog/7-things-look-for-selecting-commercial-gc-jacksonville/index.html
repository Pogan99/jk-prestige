7 Things to Look for When Selecting a Commercial GC in Jacksonville

May 4, 2026 · 7 min read · JK Prestige Constructor Corp

Jacksonville's construction growth has attracted contractors from across Florida and from other Southeast markets. Not all of them are equipped for commercial work. The state licensing system, bonding requirements, and OSHA compliance standards that apply to commercial construction are different from residential, and the consequences of hiring an underqualified GC on a commercial project — schedule overruns, code violations, failed inspections, worker injuries — fall directly on the owner. Knowing how to evaluate a commercial GC before you sign a contract is one of the highest-return decisions you can make on a project.

JK Prestige Construction has completed 500+ commercial projects in Jacksonville and Northeast Florida since 2017. The seven criteria below are what we would tell an owner to verify before signing with any GC — including us. We meet all seven. Review the JK Way to see how each applies to how we work.

1. Florida CGC License — Not a Residential License

Florida issues multiple contractor license types. A CGC (Certified General Contractor) license authorizes construction of unlimited commercial and residential projects statewide. A CBC (Certified Building Contractor) license covers a broader scope than residential but has limitations. A residential contractor license — CRC (Certified Residential Contractor) or registered residential — does not authorize commercial work. In Jacksonville's hot market, contractors with residential licenses are taking on commercial jobs. Verify your GC's license through the Florida Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR) contractor search before signing anything. Ask for the license number and confirm it is active and in good standing. A suspended or conditionally active license is a red flag that can halt your project mid-construction.

2. Commercial Project References in Your Specific Building Type

Office construction, warehouse construction, medical construction, and retail construction are different disciplines. A GC with 20 restaurant build-outs and no warehouse experience is not the right choice for a 150,000-square-foot logistics facility. Ask for three references from projects of similar type and scale — not just company names, but specific contacts at the owner or developer who managed the relationship. Call them. Ask about schedule adherence, change order frequency, and whether the principal or a junior project manager was involved day-to-day. Jacksonville's commercial market is small enough that most owners know each other — a GC with real experience in your building type will have references who answer their phones. JK can provide references from warehouse, office, medical, and healthcare projects completed in Duval, Nassau, and St. Johns counties.

3. Self-Perform Capability for at Least Framing and Drywall

Framing and drywall are the backbone of most commercial interiors. A GC that subcontracts both trades has no direct control over the two trades most likely to drive schedule delay in Jacksonville's current labor market. Specialty subs in these categories are booking 8–12 weeks out in Duval County. A self-perform GC — one that carries its own framing and drywall crews — can mobilize when the permit is issued and control quality without a sub's schedule constraints. Ask any GC candidate how they staff framing and drywall: are those crews direct employees, or 1099 labor? Do they carry workers' compensation on those crews? A GC that uses 1099 labor as "self-perform" is carrying subcontractor risk without the benefits of true self-performance. JK's framing and drywall crews are direct-hire employees under our workers' compensation policy.

4. Bonding Capacity for Projects Over $500,000

A performance bond guarantees that if your GC fails to complete the project, the surety will step in to finish it. For commercial projects above $500,000, bonding is standard practice and often required by lenders. Ask any GC candidate for their bonding company name and their single-project and aggregate bonding capacity. A GC that cannot bond a $2 million commercial project either has financial issues or has not built the bonding relationship that comes from consistent project delivery. Bonding capacity is also a proxy for financial stability — surety companies underwrite GCs carefully, and a strong bond limit indicates a GC with clean financials, documented project history, and a track record of project completion. Contact JK to request bonding documentation for your project.

5. OSHA 30 Certification on Supervisory Staff

OSHA 30 (Occupational Safety and Health Administration 30-hour construction training) is the supervisory-level safety standard for commercial construction. It is not legally required on all projects, but it is the baseline expectation for any GC working on projects above $500,000, on hospital sites, or in any municipality that has adopted enhanced safety standards. Ask specifically whether the superintendent who will be on your site daily holds OSHA 30 certification — not just whether someone in the company does. JK requires OSHA 30 certification for all supervisory personnel on commercial projects. Our crews are also ICRA-trained for healthcare construction, which is a separate, specialized certification not found at most Jacksonville GCs. Unverified safety credentials are a liability exposure for the owner as well as the GC.

6. Principal Involvement — Who Actually Manages Your Project

The person who sells you the project is often not the person who builds it. At larger commercial GCs, a senior estimator or business development manager presents, wins the job, and hands it to a project manager who may be managing 6–8 other projects simultaneously. That PM is not visiting your site daily. They are reviewing a schedule in a conference room. The question to ask: will the owner or principal of this company be involved in my project, and at what frequency? At JK, the founder answers every call and walks every site. That is not a marketing claim — it is the operating model. For owners building in Jacksonville, that level of principal access is meaningful when a problem needs a decision, not a chain of approvals.

7. Fixed-Price Contract Capability

Time and materials (T&M) commercial construction contracts routinely overrun their initial estimates by 20–40%. That range is not exceptional — it is the documented average on T&M commercial projects where scope was not fully defined at contract execution. A GC that offers only T&M pricing is either unwilling to commit to a scope or unable to price one accurately. Fixed-price contracts require a GC to understand the scope thoroughly, price it accurately, and absorb cost risk within the agreed amount. That discipline produces better preconstruction, better scope documentation, and fewer surprises. JK issues fixed-price contracts on qualifying commercial projects with a 48-hour estimate turnaround. FAQ

How do I verify a contractor's license in Florida?

Use the Florida Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR) online license verification tool at myfloridalicense.com. Search by contractor name or license number. Confirm the license type (CGC, CBC, or residential), expiration date, and any disciplinary history. A license in "null and void" or "delinquent" status cannot legally operate as GC of record on your project.

What is the difference between a CGC and a CBC license in Florida?

A CGC (Certified General Contractor) license authorizes unlimited commercial and residential construction statewide. A CBC (Certified Building Contractor) license has similar scope but carries some limitations in specific project types. Both are above residential contractor licenses. For most commercial projects in Jacksonville, the CGC is the appropriate credential. Verify that your GC holds the right license for your project type.

Is OSHA 30 required for commercial construction in Jacksonville?

OSHA 30 is not universally required by Florida law for all commercial projects, but it is required on federal projects, on many hospital and school projects by contract, and is standard practice for responsible commercial GCs. Jacksonville-area owners should require OSHA 30 certification from the superintendent assigned to their project as a condition of contract.

Why do T&M contracts overrun more than fixed-price contracts?

Time and materials contracts shift cost risk to the owner. Every decision about scope, material selection, and crew hours has a direct cost impact that the owner absorbs. Without fixed-price discipline, scope creep and inefficiency go unmanaged. Fixed-price contracts incentivize the GC to plan thoroughly, purchase materials efficiently, and complete work on schedule because cost overruns reduce the GC's margin, not the owner's budget.
