How Jacksonville Custom Home Builders Handle Long-Lead Materials Before Ground Breaks

May 4, 2026 · 8 min read · JK Prestige Constructor Corp

A custom home project that breaks ground without its long-lead materials ordered is a project waiting to stall. The scenario plays out the same way almost every time: the frame goes up, the rough-in trades begin, and then the GC identifies that the windows — which take 14 weeks to fabricate after order — were never ordered. The structural opening is framed for a unit that is not on its way. The roofing contractor cannot seal the roof deck around an opening that has no unit. Work slows in the affected zone, and the schedule impact cascades through every trade that follows.

This is not bad luck. It is a pre-construction failure: the GC did not identify long-lead items early enough to order them before construction began. A general contractor (GC) who handles this correctly builds a long-lead procurement schedule in pre-construction, before the first shovel goes in the ground, and tracks delivery dates against the construction schedule from day one.

What Long-Lead Materials Are in a Custom Home

Long-lead materials are products or components that require significant fabrication or delivery time after the purchase order is placed. In custom residential construction, the category is broader than most homeowners expect.

The primary long-lead items in a Jacksonville custom home:

Windows and exterior doors: Custom window units — aluminum-clad, fiberglass, wood-interior, or impact-rated for coastal exposure — are fabricated to order. Lead times from major manufacturers in 2025 run 10–18 weeks for standard custom configurations and up to 24 weeks for non-standard shapes, oversized units, or specialty glazing. A set of impact-rated windows for a Northeast Florida coastal home is not an off-the-shelf purchase.

Steel elements: Custom steel staircases, structural steel beams, decorative steel railing systems, and any exposed steel architectural element are fabricated in the order they are received by the steel fabricator. Lead times in 2025 run 8–14 weeks for residential custom steel, depending on fabricator backlog. Steel stairs must be sized and detailed in the architectural drawings before order — which means the GC needs a complete set of stair drawings before the order is placed, not during framing.

Custom millwork: Built-in bookshelves, custom cabinetry, paneled library walls, coffered ceiling components, and decorative beam packages are made to order in a millwork shop. Lead times from quality millwork shops in 2025 run 10–16 weeks. Cabinetry requiring shop drawings — which includes all built-in and semi-custom cabinet programs — requires an approved shop drawing set before fabrication begins, adding 2–4 weeks to the effective lead time.

Specialty roofing materials: Slate, clay tile, concrete tile, cedar shake, and certain standing seam metal roofing systems are not stocked at local distributors in project quantities. Specialty roofing lead times in 2025 run 6–14 weeks depending on material and regional availability. A roof deck that is sheathed and ready for tile cannot wait 12 weeks for the material to arrive.

Mechanical equipment: Certain HVAC equipment — geothermal heat pump units, variable refrigerant flow (VRF) systems, high-efficiency commercial-grade equipment for large homes — has lead times that have lengthened since 2021. In 2025, specialty HVAC equipment lead times in the Jacksonville market run 8–20 weeks depending on manufacturer and configuration. Standard residential split systems from Trane, Carrier, and Lennox are generally available from local distributors within 2–4 weeks.

Plumbing fixtures — specialty: Standard plumbing fixtures are stocked locally. Imported fixtures from European manufacturers (Dornbracht, Waterworks, Vola, Fantini), custom soaking tubs, large-format stone vessel sinks, and any fixture requiring custom fabrication carry lead times of 10–20 weeks.

What Happens When Long-Lead Items Are Not Identified in Pre-Construction

The consequences of discovering a long-lead problem mid-construction depend on where the project is in the construction sequence.

Discovered before framing begins: If the GC realizes that windows were not ordered at the point where the foundation is complete and framing is about to start, the window order can be placed and framing can proceed for 10–12 weeks on the interior structure and other areas. The schedule delay may be absorbed if the project was planned with buffer.

Discovered during framing: If the framing crew is setting the window rough openings and the window units are not on order, the project is in trouble. Framing can continue around other areas, but the exterior envelope — sheathing, housewrap, window installation, exterior cladding — cannot be completed. The roof cannot be fully dried in through the affected wall sections. Schedule impact is typically 10–16 weeks for standard custom window lead times.

Discovered at or after drywall: If a specialty fixture or cabinetry item is not ordered until the project is at drywall, the schedule impact is severe. Drywall cannot be finished in rooms awaiting custom built-ins. Paint, flooring, and trim installation are blocked in those spaces. A 12-week lead time at this stage of construction means a 12-week delay to occupancy.

Cost of the delay: In Jacksonville's construction market, a 10-week schedule extension on a $1.5M custom home project costs the owner roughly $8,000–$15,000 in carrying costs (construction loan interest, temporary housing if they have sold their prior home), plus any demobilization and remobilization costs for trades who have moved to other projects while waiting.

How JK Prestige's Pre-Construction Handles Long-Lead Procurement

JK's pre-construction process includes a dedicated long-lead procurement phase that runs concurrently with design coordination and permit submittal.

Step 1 — Long-lead identification in design review. During the GC's constructability review of the architectural drawings, JK's superintendent and project principal identify every product category that carries a lead time over six weeks. This review happens at the 75% complete design document stage — before permit submittal, so that the permit drawings reflect the actually specified products.

Step 2 — Lead time confirmation from suppliers. JK contacts the actual suppliers — not a general estimate from a catalog — for lead time confirmation on each long-lead item. Lead times change with manufacturer backlog; a product that ran 10 weeks in March may run 16 weeks in August. Confirmed lead times are entered into the long-lead procurement schedule.

Step 3 — Order dates set against the construction schedule. Each long-lead item has a required-on-site date based on when it is needed in the construction sequence. Working backward from that date using the confirmed lead time, JK sets the purchase order date. If that date has already passed or is within four weeks, the item is escalated — the owner is notified and a decision on expediting or schedule adjustment is made immediately.

Step 4 — Owner selections confirmed before order. JK confirms the owner's material selections before placing any purchase order. An order placed on an assumed specification — where the owner later changes the window manufacturer, the finish, or the configuration — is a wasted purchase order and an additional lead time delay. Selection confirmation is documented in writing.

Step 5 — Delivery tracking through construction. Once ordered, each long-lead item is tracked on a delivery log updated weekly in JK's owner report. Expected delivery dates are monitored against the construction schedule. If a delivery is projected to be late, JK notifies the owner and adjusts the schedule proactively rather than reactively.

Questions to Ask a Custom Home Builder About Long-Lead Procurement

When interviewing a GC for a Jacksonville custom home project, these questions reveal whether they have a real long-lead procurement process or are planning to "figure it out as they go":

  • At what point in the design process do you identify long-lead materials?
  • Do you track lead times in writing and attach them to the construction schedule?
  • How do you handle a situation where an owner has not made a selection for a long-lead item and the order date is approaching?
  • Have you had a project delayed by a long-lead item that was not ordered in time? What happened?
  • Can you show me the long-lead procurement log from a recently completed project?

A GC with a genuine pre-construction process will answer all of these questions with specifics. A GC who plans to "work through it with the homeowner" is describing a reactive process that puts the schedule risk on you.
