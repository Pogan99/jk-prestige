How to Avoid Contractor Liens on Your Property in Florida

May 4, 2026 · 8 min read · JK Prestige Constructor Corp

Florida's Construction Lien Law — Chapter 713, Florida Statutes — is one of the most owner-adverse lien regimes in the country. A subcontractor or material supplier who was never paid by your general contractor can file a lien against your property, even if you paid your GC every invoice on time. The lien attaches to your real property title, can prevent sale or refinancing, and can ultimately result in a foreclosure action if not resolved.

Understanding how Florida's lien law works — and taking the right steps from the day construction begins — is the most effective protection available to homeowners and commercial property owners.

Step 1: Understand Florida's Notice to Owner — Every NTO Is a Legal Document

An NTO — Notice to Owner — is a written notice that subcontractors, sub-subcontractors, and material suppliers are required to serve on the property owner to preserve their lien rights. Florida law requires most parties who do not have a direct contract with the owner to send an NTO within 45 days of first furnishing labor or materials to the project.

When you receive an NTO, it means that a company other than your GC is working on or supplying your project, and that company has legally protected its right to file a lien if it does not get paid.

Receiving an NTO does not mean anything is wrong. It is a routine protective measure. What matters is what you do next.

Read every NTO carefully: who sent it, what trade or material they are supplying, and the date they first furnished to the project. Keep a log of every NTO received, dated by when you received it.

Pro tip: The NTO is also useful information. If your GC told you there would be three subcontractors and you receive NTOs from seven parties, that is a data point about project scope that warrants a conversation with your GC.

---

Step 2: Respond to Every NTO by Confirming the Amount with Your GC

After receiving an NTO, contact your GC and confirm that the party who sent the NTO is correctly identified in the GC's subcontractor list, that the scope of work or supply aligns with what you approved, and that the GC's payment schedule to that party is current.

You do not respond directly to the NTO with a written document — you are verifying with your GC that the party is legitimate and that they are being paid appropriately.

If your GC cannot identify the party or cannot confirm their payment status, that is a red flag. A GC who does not know which subs are on their project or who is delinquent in payments is a GC with cash flow or management problems.

Warning: Florida law does not require you to respond to an NTO, but ignoring it means you will have no early warning if that subcontractor or supplier is not being paid. An ignored NTO often becomes a filed lien three to four months later when the owner is surprised.

---

Step 3: Require Lien Waivers from Your GC and Major Subs at Each Payment Milestone

A lien waiver is a written document in which a contractor, subcontractor, or supplier waives their right to file a lien against your property, either for an amount already paid (a conditional waiver pending payment clearing) or for an amount already received (an unconditional waiver).

Florida Statutes Section 713.20 specifies four standard forms of lien waiver that must be used in Florida. Do not accept non-standard forms — they may not be legally effective.

At every scheduled payment milestone — typically tied to project completion percentages or specific phase completions — require:

  • A conditional lien waiver from your GC for the current payment amount
  • Unconditional lien waivers from major subcontractors and suppliers for amounts paid in the prior period

Do not release payment without receiving the waivers first. This is non-negotiable. A GC who resists providing lien waivers at payment milestones is a GC who may have unpaid downstream obligations.

Step 4: File a Notice of Commencement Before Construction Begins

A Notice of Commencement — NOC — is a recorded public document that establishes the legal start date of your construction project and identifies the owner, GC, and the property being improved. It must be recorded in the official public records of the county where the property is located before construction begins.

The NOC is critical because it establishes the 45-day NTO window for subcontractors and suppliers. Without a recorded NOC, the window for those parties to serve NTOs — and therefore to preserve their lien rights — may be calculated differently, creating longer exposure periods for the owner.

In Duval County, the NOC is recorded with the Clerk of Courts. JK Prestige Construction files the NOC on every project before the first crew arrives. If your current GC has not mentioned the NOC, ask specifically whether it has been filed and recorded, and request a copy with the recording stamp.

Warning: Florida law requires that a copy of the recorded NOC be posted at the job site. If your GC is not displaying the NOC, they may not have recorded it. Unrecorded NOCs create owner exposure.

---

Step 5: Use Joint Checks to Subcontractors If You Suspect GC Cash Flow Problems

A joint check is a payment instrument made out to both the GC and a specific subcontractor or supplier. Because both parties must endorse the check, the funds cannot be diverted by the GC to cover unrelated obligations — the money goes directly to the party performing the work.

Joint checks are appropriate when: an NTO party has told you directly that they are not being paid, your GC has requested payment acceleration that does not match project progress, or you have received multiple NTOs from parties who express concern about payment.

Joint checks require advance coordination with your GC. Most reputable GCs will accept joint checks to subs when an owner has legitimate concerns about payment flow. A GC who refuses joint checks to subs when asked is refusing to allow payment accountability — that refusal itself is informative.

Pro tip: You can also use two-party joint checks at the start of a project for all major sub payments, not just as a remedial measure. Some sophisticated owners use joint checks from day one as a standard protocol, particularly on projects over $500,000.

---

Step 6: Verify That the Payment Schedule Aligns with Work Completed

Never pay ahead of progress. Paying for work that has not yet been performed — or releasing retainage before substantial completion — transfers your primary leverage to the GC at exactly the moment when your risk of non-completion is highest.

Your payment schedule should be tied to verified, measurable completion milestones. For residential projects, common milestones include: foundation complete, framing complete, rough mechanicals complete, drywall complete, and substantial completion. Each payment should be released only after the milestone is physically verified — either by you, your owner's representative, or an independent inspector.

Compare the payment schedule in your contract against actual progress at each milestone. If the GC requests payment for "75% complete" and a site visit shows 55% complete, do not release the 75% payment. Require the GC to document the completion percentage with photographs and, if necessary, a third-party inspector's confirmation.

Warning: A GC in financial distress will often accelerate payment requests while slowing progress. The pattern to watch for: payment requests come in faster than the schedule calls for, while site progress slows. This pattern often precedes a project abandonment or a dispute over final payment.

---

Step 7: If a Lien Is Filed, Respond Within 20 Days with a Demand for Statement of Account

If a lien is filed against your property despite your precautions, you have a specific legal remedy available: a demand for statement of account. Under Florida Statute 713.16, you can serve a written demand on the lienor requiring them to provide a written statement of the amount claimed, including the basis for the claim.

The lienor must respond within 30 days. If they do not respond, the lien may be unenforceable. If they respond with an amount different from the lien claim, that discrepancy is useful in any subsequent dispute.

More broadly: engage a Florida construction attorney the day a lien is filed. Do not wait. A lien transfers to a lawsuit if not responded to within the applicable statutory period, and the timeline moves quickly. In Florida, a lienor must file suit to enforce a lien within one year of recording.

The cost to dispute a lien that was not properly served, was filed after the statutory period, or lacks proper documentation is often a fraction of the cost of letting the lien proceed unchallenged.

FAQ

Can a subcontractor lien my property even if I paid my GC? Yes. Florida's Construction Lien Law allows subcontractors and suppliers who were not paid by the GC to file liens against the owner's property, even if the owner paid the GC in full. This is why lien waivers from the GC alone are insufficient — you need waivers that confirm downstream parties have been paid.

What is the difference between a conditional and unconditional lien waiver? A conditional waiver states that the party waives their lien rights conditional upon the identified payment clearing the bank. An unconditional waiver states that the payment has already been received and cleared, and the lien rights are waived without condition. Always receive a conditional waiver at the time of payment, then follow up to collect the unconditional waiver once the payment clears.

Does a Notice of Commencement need to be recorded in Duval County specifically? Yes. For properties in Duval County, Jacksonville, the NOC must be recorded with the Duval County Clerk of Courts. For projects on properties that span county lines, or for projects in adjacent counties like Clay, St. Johns, or Nassau, the NOC must be recorded in the county where the property is located.

How long is a Notice of Commencement valid? One year from the recording date, unless a different expiration period is specified in the NOC itself. If your project will extend beyond one year, the NOC must be renewed before it expires or the lien rights framework resets.

What happens if my GC files bankruptcy during the project? If your GC enters bankruptcy, any unpaid subcontractors and suppliers retain their lien rights against your property independent of the bankruptcy proceeding. Subcontractor liens survive GC bankruptcy. This is precisely why payment controls, joint checks, and lien waivers at every milestone matter — your exposure does not disappear when the GC has a financial crisis.
