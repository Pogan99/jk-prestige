How Florida's Lien Law Works — and How a Fixed-Price Contract Protects the Owner

May 4, 2026 · 7 min read · JK Prestige Constructor Corp

---

Florida's Construction Lien Law is one of the most owner-unfriendly aspects of the state's construction regulatory framework — if you don't understand it going in. The fundamental rule is this: if you hire a general contractor (GC), that GC hires subcontractors and suppliers, and any of those parties don't get paid, they can legally place a claim of lien against your property.

The critical point: your payment to the GC doesn't protect you. If you paid your GC $300,000 and the GC paid the framing sub $40,000 but kept $10,000 intended for the concrete supplier, that concrete supplier can lien your property for the $10,000 the GC didn't pass along. You paid. You can still owe.

This post explains how Florida lien law works under Chapter 713 of the Florida Statutes, the four-step protection framework that limits your exposure, and how a fixed-price contract with documented scope adds a layer of accountability that reduces lien risk.

---

How Florida's Construction Lien Law Operates

Florida's Construction Lien Law (Chapter 713, Florida Statutes) gives anyone who improves real property — contractors, subcontractors, sub-subcontractors, laborers, and material suppliers — the right to claim a lien against the property if they aren't paid for their contribution.

Who can lien: GCs, subcontractors, sub-subcontractors, laborers, architects, engineers (for construction-phase services), and material suppliers. This is a broad list. The concrete ready-mix company, the lumber yard, the drywall supply house — each can lien your property if they furnished materials to your project and weren't paid.

What a lien does: A recorded claim of lien (also called a construction lien or mechanic's lien in other states) attaches to the real property — not to the GC's business, not to the GC personally. The lien encumbers your title. If you try to sell or refinance, the lien must be resolved before closing. If unresolved, the lienholder can eventually foreclose on the lien — forcing a sale of the property to satisfy the claim.

Lien rights have time limits: The lien must be recorded within 90 days of the last date the claimant performed work or furnished materials. The lienholder must then file a lawsuit to enforce the lien within one year of recording (with some exceptions). Missing these deadlines extinguishes the lien right.

---

The Preliminary Notice: Your Early Warning System

Florida Chapter 713 requires that any party other than the GC who wants to preserve lien rights must serve a "Notice to Owner" on the property owner within 45 days of first furnishing labor, materials, or services to the project.

This notice doesn't mean the recipient won't get paid — it's a preservation requirement. But for the property owner, it functions as an early warning system:

When you receive a Notice to Owner from a party you've never met — the concrete block supplier, a roofing material distributor, a plumbing supply house — you now know three things:

  1. That party is delivering materials or services to your project
  2. They have active lien rights
  3. If they don't get paid, your property is at risk

This information lets you act before a lien is recorded: ask your GC whether that party has been paid, request proof of payment, or withhold your next GC draw until you have confirmation.

A properly recorded Notice of Commencement (NOC) is what triggers the Notice to Owner obligation. Without a recorded NOC, the lien chain is murkier. This is one of the reasons Florida law requires the NOC to be filed before construction starts.

---

The Four-Step Owner Protection Framework

Step 1: File the Notice of Commencement Before Work Begins

The NOC is the legal foundation of the lien protection system. File it with the Duval County Clerk of Court before the first crew mobilizes. Post a certified copy at the jobsite. JK Prestige handles this as part of every pre-construction process.

Without the NOC, parties who deliver materials or services may not be required to serve a Notice to Owner within the 45-day window — meaning you lose your early warning system.

Step 2: Require Lien Waivers With Every Draw

A lien waiver is a signed document from a contractor, subcontractor, or supplier confirming that they've been paid for their work through a specific date and releasing their lien rights for the amount paid.

There are two types of lien waivers:

Conditional lien waiver: Release of lien rights is conditional on the payment actually clearing. Used when a payment is issued but before it's confirmed received. If the check doesn't clear, the lien rights revive.

Unconditional lien waiver: Release of lien rights without condition. Used after payment is confirmed received. This is the final protection for the owner.

Best practice: with every draw payment to the GC, require a conditional lien waiver from the GC covering all work and materials through that date, plus conditional lien waivers from every major subcontractor and supplier that the GC has paid. Replace conditional with unconditional waivers once payments are confirmed.

JK Prestige provides lien waivers from all major subcontractors and suppliers with every draw request. This is a standard JK Prestige operating procedure — not a special request. The JK Way includes complete lien waiver documentation at every draw milestone.

Step 3: Consider Joint Checks for Large Sub Payments

For major subcontractors — MEP (Mechanical, Electrical, and Plumbing) contractors, structural steel fabricators, elevator contractors — where a single payment is large and the lien exposure is significant, joint check arrangements provide additional protection.

A joint check is a payment check made out to both the GC and the subcontractor. The sub must endorse the check to receive payment — creating a paper trail that the sub was paid directly from the owner's funds. This eliminates the risk that the GC received the sub's payment and didn't pass it along.

Joint checks require the GC's cooperation and should be negotiated as a contract term before construction, not after a problem arises.

Step 4: Use a Title Company for Large Projects

For significant residential projects — full home gut-renos, new construction — involving title company or construction escrow services adds an institutional layer of protection. The title company holds funds, verifies that lien waivers are in place before releases, and maintains a formal draw process.

Construction escrow is common in commercial real estate financing and increasingly used in high-value residential construction. The title company's involvement doesn't eliminate lien risk, but it does add professional oversight to the payment process.

---

How a Fixed-Price Contract Reduces Lien Risk

A fixed-price contract doesn't eliminate lien rights — any sub or supplier on the project retains lien rights regardless of the GC's contract structure. But a fixed-price contract with full scope documentation reduces lien risk in two specific ways.

Payment accountability. In a fixed-price contract with a milestone-based payment schedule, payments are tied to defined work completions. The GC can only draw when milestones are verified. This limits the risk that the GC is significantly ahead of their payment obligations to subs — a common trigger for GC cash flow problems that lead to unpaid subs.

Scope documentation creates liability. A fully documented scope — every line item, every trade, every sub — creates a record of which subcontractors are on the project. When combined with a lien waiver requirement for each draw, the owner has a documented record of each party's payment status. A GC who knows the owner is tracking sub payments through lien waivers has less ability to divert payment.

A cost-plus or poorly documented contract, by contrast, leaves the owner dependent on the GC's representations about who is on the project and who is getting paid.

---

What to Do If a Lien Is Filed Against Your Property

If a Notice to Owner arrives (not a lien — just the preliminary notice), act immediately: confirm with your GC that the party has been engaged and paid, or request proof of payment.

If a claim of lien is actually recorded against your property:

  1. Contact a Florida construction attorney immediately — lien enforcement timelines are unforgiving
  2. Review your payment records and lien waivers to understand your position
  3. Determine whether the GC failed to pay a sub you paid the GC to pay — this may give you a claim against the GC
  4. Options for resolving an outstanding lien include paying the claimant directly, bonding off the lien (replacing the property as security with a payment bond), or litigating the lien's validity
