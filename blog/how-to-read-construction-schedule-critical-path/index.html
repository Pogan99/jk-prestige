How to Read a Construction Schedule: Critical Path Explained

May 4, 2026 · 8 min read · JK Prestige Constructor Corp

A construction schedule handed to an owner without explanation is a document that protects the GC, not the owner. The bars look organized. The dates look reasonable. What the chart does not show you -- unless you know how to read it -- is which activities control the entire project, which ones have room to slip, and which delays are already locked in two months before they show up on the completion date.

This guide explains the critical path method in plain terms, using the kind of project context you encounter in Jacksonville commercial and residential construction.

JK Prestige Construction uses the Last Planner System (LPS) and 6-week look-aheads on all projects. This guide explains how to make sense of both.

Step 1: Understand the Critical Path (Longest Chain of Dependent Activities)

The critical path is the sequence of activities that determines the project's minimum completion date. Every activity on the critical path must finish on time for the project to finish on time. A one-day delay to any critical path activity delays the project end date by one day.

The critical path is not the most expensive work. It is not the most visible work. It is the chain of activities with zero float (explained in Step 2) that runs from project start to project finish.

Example: On a ground-up warehouse project in Duval County, the critical path might run: site clearing, grading, underground utilities, slab pour, steel erection, metal deck, roofing, exterior skin, interior rough-in, inspections, finishes, certificate of occupancy. Each activity depends on the one before it. Miss the slab pour date by two weeks and you push the CO date by two weeks, assuming nothing else changes.

Pro tip: Ask your GC to highlight or color-code the critical path on the schedule they give you. If the GC says "everything is on the critical path," that is not an answer -- it means they have not done the scheduling work or they do not want you to know.

Step 2: Identify Float (Activities with Flexibility)

Float, also called slack, is the amount of time an activity can be delayed without affecting the project completion date. An activity with 10 days of float can start 10 days late without pushing the schedule -- assuming nothing else changes.

Total float measures how much an activity can slip before it affects the project end date.

Free float measures how much an activity can slip before it affects the start of the next activity in its chain.

Why this matters for owners:

  • Float is a resource, not a guarantee. A GC can burn through float by making resource allocation decisions that delay non-critical activities.
  • Subcontractors working on float activities sometimes get pulled to critical path activities, which can turn a float activity into a critical one.
  • When the schedule is updated monthly, activities that had float in month one may be on the critical path by month three.

Warning: A schedule that shows no float anywhere is either extremely well-executed or unrealistic. Most real projects have some float, particularly in early phases. If every activity shows zero float from day one, the schedule may be constructed to make everything look urgent rather than to reflect actual dependency logic.

Step 3: Read Activity Dependencies (What Cannot Start Until Something Else Finishes)

Every activity in a construction schedule has at least one predecessor -- an activity that must finish (or start) before it can begin. These relationships are called dependencies or logic ties.

The four types:

  • Finish-to-Start (FS): Activity B cannot start until Activity A finishes. The most common type. Rough framing must finish before insulation can start.
  • Start-to-Start (SS): Activity B cannot start until Activity A starts. Plumbing rough-in and HVAC rough-in might start simultaneously in different zones.
  • Finish-to-Finish (FF): Activity B cannot finish until Activity A finishes. Punch list walkthrough cannot finish until all trade punch items are complete.
  • Start-to-Finish (SF): Rare. Activity B cannot finish until Activity A starts.

For owners, the key question at any site visit is: what activity is blocking what? If roofing is waiting for structural steel, and structural steel is waiting for a fabrication release, and the fabrication release is waiting for a stamped shop drawing, you have a chain of dependencies that may already be six weeks behind -- even if the roofing date is still three months away.

Pro tip: Ask your GC to walk you through the critical path dependencies on a single screen. If they cannot explain what is blocking what in plain language, they do not fully understand their own schedule.

Step 4: Spot the High-Risk Activities (Long-Lead Materials, Permit-Dependent Activities)

Not all activities carry the same risk. Two categories carry disproportionate schedule risk on every project:

Long-lead materials: Items with procurement times of 12 weeks or more -- structural steel, aluminum storefronts, elevator equipment, electrical switchgear, roofing systems, specialty mechanical equipment. If these items are not ordered at or before contract award, they almost certainly end up on the critical path.

Ask your GC for the long-lead procurement log. It should show: item, required on-site date, procurement start date, vendor commitment date, and current status. If this log does not exist, your long-lead risk is unmanaged.

Permit-dependent activities: In Jacksonville, commercial building permit review through the City of Jacksonville (COJAX) Building Inspection Division typically takes 4 to 8 weeks for commercial projects. No permit, no work. If the permit application was submitted late or if there are plan review comments outstanding, every activity that requires the permit is blocked.

Ask your GC: "Is the permit in hand? If not, when was it submitted and what is the current review status?"

Warning: A schedule that shows framing starting on a date that is less than 6 weeks from today, when the permit has not yet been submitted, is a schedule that will slip.

Step 5: What a 6-Week Look-Ahead Shows vs. the Master Schedule

The master schedule is the project's full timeline from mobilization to CO, typically spanning months or years. It is built at a summary level -- activities may represent weeks of work.

The 6-week look-ahead is a short-interval schedule that shows what is actually planned in the next six weeks, at a task-by-task level. It is the document where real field planning happens.

JK Prestige uses a 6-week look-ahead on all projects because:

  • It forces the field team to confirm that resources (crews, materials, equipment) are actually available for each planned task
  • It surfaces constraints (material deliveries, inspections, subcontractor availability) while there is still time to address them
  • It connects the master schedule to daily field work

The 6-week look-ahead should be updated weekly, not monthly. An outdated look-ahead is a planning failure.

As an owner, you can use the look-ahead to check: "Is the activity that was planned for this week actually complete? If not, why not, and what is the recovery?"

Pro tip: Compare the look-ahead from three weeks ago to the current schedule. Activities that were "planned" three weeks ago and are still "planned" this week -- without explanation -- indicate a chronic planning problem.

Step 6: Three Warning Signs the Schedule Is Slipping Before It Becomes a Crisis

Schedule problems become visible on the master schedule long after they could have been addressed. Here are three earlier indicators:

1. Look-ahead completion rates below 80%: If the GC's 6-week look-ahead shows activities planned for the week and fewer than 80% are actually completed each week, the project is losing ground. This is the Last Planner System metric called Percent Plan Complete (PPC). A PPC below 70% for two consecutive weeks is a serious warning sign.

2. Long-lead items not yet ordered: If the schedule shows structural steel erection in 14 weeks and you cannot confirm that steel has been ordered from a fabricator, the erection date is fiction. Ask for vendor confirmation in writing.

3. Inspection requests not yet submitted: Building department inspections in Jacksonville require advance notice -- typically 24 to 48 hours for residential, longer for commercial. If a rough framing inspection is scheduled for Friday but the GC has not yet called it in, Friday's inspection is not happening.

Warning: A GC who responds to schedule concern questions with "we'll make it up" without specifying how has not identified a recovery plan. "Making it up" requires either additional crews (which cost money) or working nights and weekends (which also cost money and may require owner authorization under the contract).

Step 7: How to Ask Your GC About Schedule Risk

Most owners ask "are we on schedule?" and accept a yes or no. Here are more useful questions:

  • "Which activities currently have zero float?"
  • "What is our Percent Plan Complete for the last three weeks?"
  • "Are there any long-lead materials without a confirmed delivery date within the next 8 weeks?"
  • "Are there any open RFIs (Requests for Information) that are blocking critical path work?"
  • "What is the current permit status for [specific phase]?"
  • "If we lose [activity X] for one week, what is the impact to the CO date?"

At JK Prestige Construction, the principal answers these questions directly -- not through a project administrator reading from a report. On home renovation projects and commercial builds, the schedule is a working document, not a sales tool.

Ask your GC for the critical path activities list at every monthly owner meeting. If the list changes significantly from month to month without explanation, the schedule is being managed reactively rather than proactively.

FAQ

H3: What software do GCs use to create construction schedules? The most common tools are Primavera P6 (for large commercial and industrial projects), Microsoft Project, and Procore's scheduling module. For smaller projects, GCs may use spreadsheet-based Gantt charts. The software matters less than whether the schedule logic is sound and whether it is updated regularly.

H3: What is the Last Planner System? The Last Planner System is a collaborative planning method developed by the Lean Construction Institute. It uses short-interval scheduling (6-week look-aheads), weekly planning commitments, and Percent Plan Complete tracking to improve schedule reliability. JK Prestige Construction uses LPS on all projects.

H3: Can I require a specific schedule format in my contract? Yes. Your contract can specify that the GC must provide a baseline schedule in a specific software format, update it at defined intervals, and maintain a 6-week look-ahead updated weekly. Include the schedule requirements in the contract documents, not just as a verbal expectation.

H3: What happens if the GC misses the substantial completion date? If your contract includes liquidated damages (LD) for late completion, the GC owes a defined daily amount for each day beyond substantial completion. If there are no LDs, your remedy is typically general damages, which requires proving actual financial harm. Know what your contract says before the project starts.

H3: How long does it take to get a commercial building permit in Jacksonville? For commercial projects in Duval County, expect 4 to 8 weeks for initial permit review through the City of Jacksonville Building Inspection Division. Projects with complex systems (fire suppression, medical gas, specialized MEP) may require additional review cycles. Factor this into the project schedule before mobilizing.
