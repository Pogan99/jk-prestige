Adding a Bathroom in Florida: What the Process Actually Looks Like

June 5, 2026 · 6 min read · JK Prestige Constructor Corp

Adding a bathroom is one of the highest-ROI renovation moves in Jacksonville. A home that goes from one full bath to two — or from two to three — sees measurable value increases and broader buyer appeal. But the process is more involved than most homeowners expect when they start calling contractors.

This guide covers what a bathroom addition actually requires in Florida — permits, rough-in, sequencing, and what the work costs.

What "Bathroom Addition" Means vs. Remodel

A bathroom addition is a new bathroom being built where no bathroom previously existed. This requires:

  • New plumbing supply lines run from existing supply
  • New drain lines extending from existing stack or running to a new tie-in point
  • New electrical circuit (GFCI required per NEC in all bathrooms)
  • Framing modifications to accommodate the new space (if converting a closet, bedroom, or unfinished area)
  • Moisture barrier, backer, and waterproofing that a simple remodel assumes are already there

A bathroom remodel is updating fixtures, tile, and finishes in an existing bathroom. Far simpler. Far less expensive.

This guide focuses on the addition — building a bathroom from scratch in a space that wasn't a bathroom before.

Florida Permitting Requirements for a Bathroom Addition

Adding a bathroom always requires permits in Florida. No exceptions. The permits required:

  • Building permit — structural work, framing changes, closet conversions
  • Plumbing permit — new supply lines, new drain lines, fixtures
  • Electrical permit — new GFCI circuit, vent fan wiring, lighting

In Jacksonville (Duval County), permit fees for a bathroom addition run approximately $500 to $1,200 depending on the scope. Permit review typically takes 2 to 5 weeks.

Any GC who tells you a bathroom addition doesn't need permits is either wrong or planning to skip them. Unpermitted bathroom additions are a significant problem at resale — they come up in buyer inspections and can require demolition and reconstruction to correct.

Where the Drain Goes: The Hardest Part of a Bathroom Addition

The most common challenge in a bathroom addition isn't the tile or the fixtures — it's getting the drain line to the right place.

A toilet drain requires a 3-inch line at minimum slope (1/4" fall per foot of run) to the main stack or a connection to the building drain. A shower or tub drain requires a 2-inch line with the same slope requirement.

In a slab-on-grade home (the majority of Jacksonville homes built after 1960), adding a bathroom where there isn't already a drain requires breaking concrete. The slab gets saw-cut, the drain line is trenched and installed, and the slab is patched. This adds $3,000 to $7,000 to the scope depending on run length and access.

In pier-and-beam homes (common in Springfield, Riverside, Avondale, and older Murray Hill), drain lines run under the floor in accessible crawl space. No concrete cutting — the drain extension is simpler and less expensive.

Bathroom Addition Cost in Jacksonville

Half bath addition (toilet + sink, converting closet or small room): $18,000 to $32,000

Includes framing, plumbing rough-in, electrical, drywall, tile, fixtures, and permit. Slab cutting adds to the lower end of this range.

Full bath addition (toilet, sink, shower or tub, ventilation): $28,000 to $52,000

Adds shower pan or tub installation, waterproofing, full tile shower surround, exhaust fan. High-end tile and custom shower work push toward the top of this range.

Master bathroom addition (full bath, separate shower and soaking tub, double vanity): $45,000 to $85,000

Premium fixture package, custom tile work, frameless glass, heated floor, separate water closet. These are master bath additions attached to primary bedroom suites, sometimes involving a small room addition or significant interior reconfiguration.

These ranges include labor and standard materials. High-end tile, custom cabinetry, and specialty fixtures add to the top of each range.

The Construction Sequence for a Bathroom Addition

This is how JK Prestige sequences a bathroom addition:

  1. Pre-construction: scope document, plumbing tie-in assessment, structural review
  2. Permit application
  3. Framing modifications (closet wall relocation, door opening, etc.)
  4. Plumbing rough-in: drain extension, supply lines, clean-out installation
  5. Electrical rough-in: new circuit, GFCI locations, exhaust fan wiring
  6. Rough inspections (plumbing and electrical rough must be inspected before close-up)
  7. Moisture barrier and cement backer installation
  8. Waterproofing (shower pan pre-slope, curb, liner or modern waterproofing membrane)
  9. Tile installation: shower, floor, surrounding surfaces
  10. Drywall and finish painting
  11. Plumbing trim-out: toilet, vanity, fixtures
  12. Electrical trim-out: outlets, switches, exhaust fan, lighting
  13. Accessories: mirror, towel bars, shower door or curtain rod
  14. Final inspections
  15. Certificate of Occupancy (if addition triggered CO requirement)

Timeline: 8 to 14 weeks for a standard full bathroom addition. Half baths run 6 to 10 weeks.
