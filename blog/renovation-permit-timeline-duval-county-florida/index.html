Renovation Permit Timeline in Duval County: What Jacksonville Homeowners Should Expect

June 7, 2026 · 6 min read · JK Prestige Constructor Corp

One of the most common surprises on a Jacksonville renovation is permit timing. A homeowner hires a GC, signs the contract, and expects work to begin in two weeks. Then they find out the permit review is 15–25 business days, the structural engineer's drawings aren't ready, and the contractor is still waiting on a trade contractor's license to clear the portal.

The permit process in Duval County is not designed to slow down construction. It exists because residential and commercial construction in Florida carries real risk — to structure, to electrical systems, to the people who live in these buildings. But understanding the timeline and what drives it makes the experience far less stressful.

Here's what to expect for the most common renovation project types in Jacksonville.

How Duval County Permits Work

All permits in Jacksonville are processed through the City of Jacksonville Building Inspection Division, accessible via the online portal at coj.net. The city uses a third-party plan review system for most residential projects.

There are two review tracks:

Standard review: Full plan review by city staff. This is required for structural changes, electrical service upgrades, HVAC system changes, and additions over a certain square footage. Standard review is 10–25 business days for residential projects, measured from the date a complete application is accepted.

Express review: For simple trades work (permit-only items, no full plan review required). Electrical panel replacements, water heater replacements, simple HVAC changeouts. These can clear in 3–7 business days.

The permit clock starts when the application is accepted as complete — not when it's submitted. A submission with missing documents (incomplete drawings, no engineer stamp, missing contractor license number) gets rejected and goes back to the applicant. That restarts the clock.

Permit Timelines by Project Type

Kitchen Gut Renovation (no addition)

Typical permit timeline: 10–18 business days

A kitchen gut renovation that stays within the existing footprint — demo down to studs, new layout, new electrical circuits, new plumbing rough-in — requires a building permit with plumbing and electrical sub-permits. If there are no structural changes, you don't need a structural engineer.

What causes delays: undersized drawings (must be to scale, typically 1/4 inch per foot), missing circuit schedule on the electrical plans, plumbing riser diagram missing.

Bathroom Addition or Remodel

Typical permit timeline: 10–18 business days

Same track as kitchen renovation. If the bathroom remodel involves moving the toilet (which affects the drain-waste-vent stack), the plumbing drawings need a riser diagram that shows the new configuration.

If you're adding a bathroom to a space that didn't previously have plumbing — converting a bedroom to a bathroom — plan for a longer review. The city will want to see how the new drain line ties into the existing stack and where it exits to the sanitary sewer.

Load-Bearing Wall Removal

Typical permit timeline: 15–25 business days

Structural reviews take longer. The plan reviewer routes structural drawings to a structural review specialist. The drawings must include the beam design, post locations, footing details, and engineer of record stamp. Any comment on the structural drawings — even a minor one — extends the timeline by another 5–10 business days for a resubmission cycle.

At JK Prestige, we engage the structural engineer before submitting the permit application. The engineer's drawings are part of the initial submission, not a late add. This single practice eliminates most of the resubmission delays we see competitors' projects experience.

Whole-House Gut Renovation

Typical permit timeline: 15–30 business days

A gut renovation affecting every system — framing, electrical, plumbing, HVAC, finishes — is treated as a major alteration. The permit package includes architectural drawings (floor plans, elevations, window/door schedule), mechanical/electrical/plumbing drawings, and structural drawings if any structural work is included.

The most common delay on gut renovations: the HVAC drawings. Many homeowners and GCs treat HVAC as a field-design trade, but Duval County requires a HVAC design drawing showing equipment sizing (Manual J or equivalent) and duct layout for new systems. If the HVAC sub doesn't provide this, the permit stalls.

Home Addition

Typical permit timeline: 20–35 business days

Additions require architectural drawings showing the new footprint, how it connects to the existing structure, and all applicable setbacks and lot coverage calculations. Wind load calculations and energy code compliance documentation (RESCHECK) are required.

Duval County's wind design requirements are dictated by the Florida Building Code and the wind speed map. Jacksonville is a 130 mph design wind speed zone for most residential applications. Every addition requires wind load documentation that meets this threshold.

Accessory Dwelling Unit (ADU) or Garage Conversion

Typical permit timeline: 20–40 business days

ADUs are treated similarly to additions but with additional review for utility connections, separate addressing (in some cases), and zoning compliance. The zoning review step — verifying that the ADU is permitted in the applicable PUD or zoning district — adds time beyond the standard building code review.

Riverside, Avondale, and Springfield homeowners should be aware that these neighborhoods have additional overlay review requirements due to historic district status. The Jacksonville Historic Preservation Commission may need to review exterior changes, which adds 15–30 business days on top of the standard permit timeline.

What Causes Permit Delays

Beyond incomplete applications, the most common causes of delay on Jacksonville renovation projects:

Contractor not licensed for the scope. The licensed GC's CGC or CBC must be on the permit. If the electrical sub pulls a separate permit, they need their own EC license. License verification happens at application, and a license number that isn't current in the state system causes an automatic rejection.

Drawings not prepared by the correct licensed professional. Structural drawings require a Florida PE stamp. Architectural drawings for projects over a certain size require a Florida RA or AI stamp. Homeowner-drawn plans on notebook paper are not acceptable.

Changes during review. If the owner or GC submits a plan change while the permit is in review, the reviewer typically has to restart from the beginning. Changes should wait until after permit issuance whenever possible.

Plan comments that require design team action. If the plan reviewer issues a comment that requires the structural engineer or architect to revise the drawings, the GC has to coordinate that revision and resubmit. Each revision cycle is 5–10 business days.

Active code enforcement violations on the property. If there are open permit violations on the property from prior work, the city may hold new permits until the violations are resolved.
