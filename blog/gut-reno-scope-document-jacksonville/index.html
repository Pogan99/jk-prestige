What a Full Home Gut-Reno Scope Document Should Include Before Any Wall Comes Down

May 4, 2026 · 9 min read · JK Prestige Constructor Corp

---

The scope document is the most important piece of paper in your gut-reno. It is the foundation of your fixed-price contract. It determines what you're getting built, what you're paying, and what your recourse is when something goes wrong. A complete scope document prevents change orders. An incomplete one causes them.

Most Jacksonville homeowners sign scope documents that aren't complete. They don't know it because they've never seen a complete one. By the time the gaps show up — usually mid-demo, when a contractor announces that something "wasn't in scope" — the leverage is gone.

This post walks through every element a full gut-reno scope document must contain. Read it before you sign anything.

---

Why the Scope Document Comes Before Demolition — Not After

The right sequence on a gut-reno is: scope document first, then demolition. Every wall that comes down before the scope is locked gives the contractor more information and you less leverage. Once your home is open to the studs, you're in a position where you almost have to approve whatever additional work the contractor identifies — because the alternative is a partially demolished house.

At JK Prestige, week one of every gut-reno is scope lock and schedule baseline. The scope document is delivered, reviewed, and agreed upon before any demolition begins. This is not a procedural formality. It is the mechanism that makes a fixed-price contract real.

A GC who performs demolition before delivering a complete scope document is using demolition to bypass the negotiation. Do not allow it.

The scope document is also what JK's fixed-price process is built on. Subsurface validation happens before the scope is finalized — precisely so that what's behind the walls doesn't become a change-order surprise after demolition begins.

---

Section 1: Demolition Scope

The demolition section must be specific about what comes out and what stays. "Demo existing kitchen" is not a demolition scope. A complete demolition scope identifies:

  • What is being removed: drywall (to studs or to framing face), flooring (to subfloor or to structure), ceilings (drywall or plaster and lath), cabinetry, counters, fixtures, MEP rough-in
  • What is staying: load-bearing walls, structural elements, fixtures in adjacent spaces, finishes in adjacent areas that are to be protected
  • Selective demo vs. full demo: if only specific areas of a room are being opened, those areas must be identified by wall, ceiling area, or floor zone
  • Asbestos and lead paint notation: older Jacksonville homes (pre-1980) may contain asbestos in floor tile, drywall texture, or pipe insulation, and lead paint in painted surfaces. The scope document should note whether testing has been completed, and if asbestos or lead is present, how abatement will be handled and who is responsible
  • Debris haul-off: who is responsible, what the dumpster placement location is, and what the haul-off schedule looks like during the project

If the demolition scope does not address what is being protected, you are relying on verbal assurances. Verbal assurances do not survive demo day.

---

Section 2: Structural Work

If structural work is in scope — wall removal, beam installation, foundation repair, joist sistering, header replacement — it must be specified in the scope document with the following detail:

  • Which walls are being removed and confirmation of whether they are load-bearing (this should be documented, not assumed)
  • Beam or header specifications for each removed wall: material (LVL, steel, dimensional lumber), size, span, and bearing point conditions
  • Permit drawings reference: structural work that requires permits must be performed from engineer-stamped drawings; the scope document should reference whether drawings are included in the contract or are the owner's responsibility
  • Foundation work: if foundation conditions are to be addressed, specify what work is being done, by whom, and what the triggering condition is

Structural work is the element most frequently discovered mid-project in older Jacksonville homes. In a proper preconstruction process, a structural assessment happens before the scope document is written — so that structural conditions are priced into the base contract, not surfaced as a change order after demo.

---

Section 3: MEP Rough-In

Mechanical, electrical, and plumbing (MEP) rough-in is the core of a gut-reno. "Replace plumbing" is not a MEP scope. A complete MEP section covers each system separately.

Plumbing

  • Supply material specified (copper, PEX, or CPVC), size, and configuration
  • Drain rough-in: cast iron or PVC, specified locations for each fixture rough-in (toilet, lavatory, tub/shower, kitchen sink, dishwasher, washing machine)
  • Water heater: type (tank or tankless), fuel (gas or electric), location, and venting
  • Any relocated fixtures: clearly identified with new rough-in location and reason

Electrical

  • Panel specification: amperage, manufacturer, number of circuits
  • New circuit list: which circuits are being added or replaced, amperage rating for each
  • Rough-in specification: wire gauge and type for each circuit category (receptacles, HVAC, kitchen dedicated circuits)
  • Lighting rough-in: location and box type for each fixture
  • Low-voltage rough-in: if security, audio, or network cabling is in scope, it should be specified here — not verbally agreed
  • Code compliance notation: specifying that all electrical work will comply with current NEC (National Electrical Code) and Florida Building Code requirements

Mechanical

  • HVAC system specification: equipment type and sizing (tons), SEER rating, fuel type
  • Duct design: supply and return duct routing, register locations
  • If existing ductwork is to be reused, its condition and the cleaning/modification scope should be noted
  • Ventilation: exhaust fans in bathrooms and kitchen, makeup air requirements

A scope document that says "replace MEP as needed" is not a MEP scope. It is permission to price MEP after the fact.

---

Section 4: Insulation

Insulation decisions in a Jacksonville gut-reno are not cosmetic. Northeast Florida's humid climate and cooling-dominated energy profile mean that insulation and air sealing directly affect your utility bills, your HVAC equipment sizing, and your indoor air quality.

The scope document should specify:

  • Insulation type and R-value for each location: exterior walls (R-13 or R-19 batt, spray foam, or rigid board), attic (R-38 or higher blown-in or batt), crawlspace or slab edge (if applicable)
  • Air sealing: rim joist sealing, top-plate sealing, penetration sealing at MEP rough-ins
  • Vapor management: in a hot-humid climate like Jacksonville, vapor barrier placement and type matters — the document should specify approach
  • Exclusions: if attic insulation is not in scope on a partial gut-reno, that exclusion should be stated

---

Section 5: Drywall

Drywall is where the gut-reno becomes visible. The scope should specify:

  • Board type and thickness: standard 1/2" on walls; 5/8" Type X at fire-rated assemblies; moisture-resistant in wet areas
  • Finish level: Level 4 is standard for residential (smooth surface for paint); Level 5 if high-gloss paint or critical lighting is planned; Level 3 is minimum for textured surfaces
  • Texture specification: orange peel, knockdown, smooth — specify which areas receive which finish
  • Ceiling height and configuration: standard flat, tray ceiling, vaulted — if framing is involved, this should also be covered in the structural section

If the drywall scope is not specified, different interpretations of "standard" will cost you money at the punchlist.

Section 6: Windows and Doors

If windows or exterior doors are being replaced, the scope must specify:

  • Manufacturer, product line, and model number (or equivalent)
  • U-factor and SHGC (Solar Heat Gain Coefficient) — Florida Building Code sets minimums for energy compliance; a higher SHGC in a Jacksonville home significantly increases cooling costs
  • Frame material (vinyl, fiberglass, aluminum-clad wood)
  • Interior and exterior trim: is trim being removed and replaced, or is existing trim being reused?
  • Whether installation includes full flashing and integration with exterior water-resistive barrier

For historic district properties in Jacksonville, window specifications must also comply with the JHPC's Certificate of Appropriateness requirements. This affects material, profile, and lite configuration.

Interior doors should specify material, pre-hung or slab, hardware schedule (or reference to a separate hardware specification), and whether existing frames are to be reused.

---

Section 7: Finishes Schedule

The finishes schedule covers the visible end of the project. This section is often where allowances appear — which is legitimate when clearly labeled. The scope should include:

  • Flooring: type (LVP, hardwood, tile, carpet), specified product or allowance, square footage, transitions, and underlayment
  • Cabinetry: specified product or allowance, door style, finish, hardware (or hardware allowance)
  • Counters: specified material and thickness, or allowance
  • Tile: specified product or allowance for each tiled area; grout color; substrate (cement board, Schluter membrane)
  • Plumbing fixtures above rough-in: fixtures (toilets, faucets, showerheads, tubs) can be owner-supplied or contractor-supplied; the scope document must specify which, and if contractor-supplied, the product or allowance
  • Lighting fixtures: same — owner-supplied vs. contractor-supplied; allowance if applicable
  • Paint: interior paint spec (sheen by area), number of coats, whether primer is included; contractor-applied or owner-applied
  • Millwork and trim: baseboard profile and height, door casing profile, crown if applicable

Every allowance line must state the dollar amount and whether it is contractor-furnished (CF), owner-furnished contractor-installed (OFCI), or owner-furnished owner-installed (OFOI). "Kitchen fixtures TBD" is not a finishes schedule.

---

Section 8: Long-Lead Items

Some materials have lead times that, if not identified before demolition, will cause schedule delays mid-project. Cabinets from certain manufacturers run 8–14 weeks. Specialty windows can run 10–16 weeks. Custom tile, appliances, and plumbing fixtures can have lead times that exceed the rough-in phase.

The scope document should identify all long-lead items with anticipated lead times. These items should be ordered before or immediately after contract signing — not during demolition.

A GC who does not raise long-lead items in the scope document is going to discover them mid-project and use them as an explanation for schedule slippage.

---

Section 9: Exclusions

What is not in the scope is as important as what is. A complete scope document contains a clear exclusions list, which should include:

  • Work in adjacent spaces not covered by the renovation
  • Owner-supplied items (appliances, light fixtures, specialty hardware)
  • Permit fees (if not included in the contract price)
  • Furniture, window treatments, and accessories
  • Landscaping, exterior painting, or site work not specifically included
  • Any work that depends on conditions not yet validated

Exclusions protect both parties. They prevent disputes about what was "supposed" to be included. Review the exclusions section carefully — anything you expect to see in the project that is not in the scope and not in the exclusions is a gap you need to resolve before signing.

---

Section 10: Contingency Allocation

A complete scope document for a gut-reno includes a disclosed contingency: a fixed percentage of the contract value (typically 5–10%) set aside for genuinely unknown conditions that could not be validated before demolition. The scope document should state:

  • The contingency dollar amount
  • What it can be used for (owner-approved unknown conditions)
  • How unused contingency is handled (returned to owner vs. applied to final payment)

A contingency is not the same as an open-ended change-order authorization. It is a pre-negotiated budget for the unknown, with a ceiling and clear rules. Any use of contingency funds should require written owner approval before work proceeds.

---

Review Your Scope Document Against This List Before Signing

Here is a quick checklist before you sign any gut-reno contract in Jacksonville:

  • [ ] Demolition scope specifies what comes out, what stays, and what is protected
  • [ ] Asbestos/lead paint status addressed
  • [ ] Structural work specified with beam/header details and permit drawing reference
  • [ ] MEP rough-in specified for plumbing, electrical, and mechanical separately
  • [ ] Insulation type, R-value, and air sealing specified by location
  • [ ] Drywall finish level and texture specified
  • [ ] Windows and doors specified with product, U-factor, and trim scope
  • [ ] Finishes schedule includes all trades with allowances clearly labeled
  • [ ] Long-lead items identified with anticipated lead times
  • [ ] Exclusions list present and reviewed
  • [ ] Contingency amount disclosed with rules for use

If more than two or three of these are missing, the scope document is not complete. Signing it transfers all that uncertainty to you in the form of future change orders.
