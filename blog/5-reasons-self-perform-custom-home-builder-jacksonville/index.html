5 Reasons to Use a Self-Perform Builder for Your Custom Home

May 4, 2026 · 6 min read · JK Prestige Constructor Corp

Most general contractors in the residential construction market do not employ trade workers directly. They manage subcontractors — separate companies who perform framing, drywall, finish carpentry, roofing, and other scopes under a subcontract agreement. The GC coordinates, schedules, and bills; the subs provide labor and materials for their trade. This model works adequately in high-volume production building where the same sub follows the same plan across 50 units simultaneously.

In custom construction, it introduces structural accountability problems at exactly the points where accountability matters most. A self-perform GC — one who employs the framing crew, the drywall crew, and the finish carpentry crew directly — is accountable in a fundamentally different way. JK Prestige Construction self-performs framing, concrete, drywall, finish carpentry, and roofing. The five differences below explain why that matters for your project specifically.

1. Schedule Accountability — The GC Cannot Blame the Sub for a Framing Delay

When a subcontracted framing crew doesn't mobilize on schedule, the GC's options are limited: call the sub, wait, negotiate, or threaten to replace them. All of those options take days. When the GC is also the framing crew, there is no intermediary — the superintendent who committed to the schedule is the superintendent who controls the labor. On a Jacksonville custom home with a 12-month construction timeline, framing typically represents 6 to 10 weeks of activity. A two-week framing delay cascades into a two-week delay in rough mechanical inspection, a two-week delay in insulation, and a two-week delay in drywall. In Northeast Florida's custom market, where owner move-in dates are tied to school calendars, lease expirations, and real estate closings, two weeks is not a trivial number.

2. Quality Continuity — The Same Superintendent Who Sets the Framing Inspects the Drywall

In a subcontractor model, the framing crew finishes and leaves. The drywall crew arrives weeks later and builds on whatever the framing crew left behind. When the drywall crew finds a wall that is 3/8 inch out of plumb in a 6-foot run, they have two choices: report it and delay the job while the framing sub returns to fix it, or float the joint and hope the finish conceals it. In practice, they often choose the second option. When framing and drywall are both self-performed by the same company — with the same superintendent accountable for both scopes — the incentive structure changes. The framing superintendent knows they will also be responsible for the drywall installation quality. Catching framing tolerance issues before drywall is always easier and less expensive than discovering them after.

3. Cost Transparency — No Sub Markup on the Two Largest Cost Categories

In a subcontractor model, the GC marks up each subcontractor's invoice — typically 10 to 20% — as their management fee for that scope. On a $60,000 framing subcontract and a $35,000 drywall subcontract, a 15% markup adds $14,250 to the project cost. That cost does not go into materials, labor skill, or any tangible quality input — it pays the GC for the administrative work of managing a subcontractor they do not employ. A self-perform GC eliminates that markup on the trades they self-perform: the full dollar goes to labor, materials, tools, and equipment. In Jacksonville's current construction market, where custom home budgets are under pressure from elevated material costs, this is a meaningful difference — particularly when framing and drywall together represent 20 to 30% of total construction cost.

4. Faster Mobilization — Own Crew Starts Monday, Not When the Sub Has Capacity

Custom construction in Northeast Florida often works around tight permit timelines. When a permit issues, the first trade on site is typically framing. A GC with their own framing crew can mobilize within days of permit issuance. A GC who relies on a subcontractor must wait until that sub's current project is complete — or until the sub has a crew available. In Jacksonville's active construction market, framing subs with strong track records carry backlog. A self-perform framing crew has no competing project to finish first: they are employees, not an independent business managing their own schedule. For an owner who has waited through a 6-week permit review by Duval County Building Services or St. Johns County, a 3-week wait for a framing sub to become available adds real timeline pain.

5. Warranty Accountability — One Responsible Party for All Self-Performed Scopes

Post-construction warranty claims in a subcontractor model frequently devolve into a trade-by-trade liability dispute. A crack at the intersection of a window rough opening and the drywall field may involve the framing sub (rough opening too tight), the window installer (improper shimming), and the drywall sub (taped over a live joint). Each party points to another. The GC coordinates the dispute but may not have direct authority to compel any of the subs to return. When framing, finish carpentry, and drywall are all self-performed, one party is responsible for all three scopes — and that party employs the crew that can return to make the repair. JK Prestige Construction's self-perform model means callbacks on the trades we perform are resolved internally, on our schedule, without a subcontractor coordination loop.

FAQ

What trades does JK Prestige self-perform on a Jacksonville custom home?

JK self-performs framing, concrete, drywall, finish carpentry, and roofing. Mechanical, electrical, and plumbing (MEP) trades are performed by licensed subcontractors, which is standard practice for licensed GCs in Florida — state licensing for electrical, plumbing, and HVAC is trade-specific and separate from a general contractor license.

Does self-performing trades reduce cost compared to using subcontractors?

It eliminates the markup on those trades, which reduces cost on those specific scopes. Total project cost also depends on material pricing, site conditions, and finish specifications. The cost difference is real — typically 10 to 20% on framing and drywall combined — but it should not be the only reason to select a self-perform GC. Schedule reliability and warranty accountability are equally important.

How can I verify that a GC actually self-performs vs. claims to but uses subs?

Ask to see payroll records or W-2 documentation for the framing crew. A true self-perform operation employs the workers — they appear on payroll, not as 1099 subcontractors. You can also visit the site during framing and ask who the workers' employer is. In Florida, workers are required to carry proof of employment or be covered under the GC's workers' compensation policy.

Do Florida building codes allow a self-perform GC to do their own roofing?

Yes. A Florida-licensed general contractor (CGC or CBC) can perform roofing work on projects they are also the GC for, without a separate roofing contractor license, under Florida Statute 489.105. This is the structure under which JK performs roofing on its own projects. JK also holds a GAF Master Elite designation for roofing quality, which is a manufacturer-level certification separate from state licensing.
