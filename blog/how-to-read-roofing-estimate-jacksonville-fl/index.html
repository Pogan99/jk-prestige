How to Read a Roofing Estimate in Jacksonville FL: Line Items That Should Never Be Missing

May 4, 2026 · 7 min read · JK Prestige Constructor Corp

---

A complete roofing estimate for a Jacksonville home lists every material, every labor item, and every permit fee on a separate line. If you receive a single-number bid — "replace your roof for $14,000" — you have no basis for comparison, and no protection when disputes arise. This post walks through every line item a legitimate estimate must include, what each one covers, and which omissions signal a contractor you should not hire.

Roof Replacement Jacksonville: What a Complete Estimate Looks Like

A proper roofing estimate is not a one-page summary. For a standard Jacksonville residential tear-off and replacement on a 2,000 sqft home, the line-item list should run 12 to 15 items before labor. Each line tells you what the contractor is installing, what grade, and what it costs separately.

1. Tear-Off and Disposal Fee

This line covers removing your existing roofing material down to the deck. In Jacksonville, most residential jobs involve one existing shingle layer. The fee should specify:

  • Number of layers being removed
  • Haul-off and dumpster placement (some contractors add this separately)
  • Per-square pricing (a "square" = 100 sqft of roofing)

A typical tear-off on a 20-square residential roof in Northeast Florida runs $300–$600 for disposal alone. If this is not a separate line, you cannot verify you are being charged a fair rate — or that removal is included at all.

2. Decking Inspection and Replacement Allowance

The decking allowance is one of the most frequently gamed line items in roofing estimates. The deck is the plywood or OSB sheathing beneath everything else. In Jacksonville's humid climate, decking rot from long-term leaks is common.

A complete estimate lists:

  • A per-sheet price for replacement (typically $75–$125 per 4x8 sheet of 7/16" OSB in Jacksonville)
  • An estimated allowance (e.g., "5 sheets included; additional sheets at $90/sheet")

If a bid simply says "replace damaged decking as needed" without a per-unit price, you have no cost ceiling. That is a contractual exposure.

---

Underlayment, Ice and Water Shield, and Drip Edge

3. Underlayment Type and Weight

Underlayment is the moisture barrier installed directly over the deck before shingles go on. There are two main categories:

  • Felt underlayment: 15 lb or 30 lb. The 30 lb is more durable and required in some Florida Building Code applications.
  • Synthetic underlayment: lighter, stronger, and better in Florida's heat. Brands include GAF FeltBuster, CertainTeed DiamondDeck.

An estimate that says "underlayment" without specifying type and weight is unverifiable. You cannot confirm after installation what was used.

4. Ice and Water Shield at Penetrations

Florida does not require ice and water shield (self-adhering membrane) across full decks the way northern climates do, but the Florida Building Code does require it at penetrations, valleys, and eave edges in certain wind-speed zones. Jacksonville sits in a high-wind zone. The estimate should specify:

  • Linear footage or square footage of ice and water shield
  • Installation locations (valleys, around skylights, chimney base, pipe boots)

5. Drip Edge — Code-Required in Florida

Drip edge is the metal flashing installed at the roof perimeter that directs water away from the fascia. It is not optional. The Florida Building Code requires drip edge on all residential roofs, specified in Florida Building Code Section R905.2.8.5.

The estimate should list:

  • Linear footage of drip edge
  • Material gauge (aluminum or galvanized steel)
  • Whether it is installed under or over the underlayment at eaves vs. rakes (code specifies both)

A bid that bundles drip edge into a generic "flashing" line or omits it entirely is a red flag.

Flashing, Ventilation, and Penetration Details

6. Flashing — All Five Types

Flashing is the waterproofing metal at every roof-to-wall junction, valley, and penetration. A complete estimate lists each type separately:

  • Step flashing: L-shaped pieces at sidewall-to-roof intersections, installed one per shingle course
  • Counter flashing: the overlapping layer that covers step flashing at masonry walls
  • Chimney flashing: base flashing, step flashing, counter flashing, and cricket (saddle) if the chimney is wider than 30"
  • Pipe boots: rubber or lead boot around plumbing vents — one per pipe, sized to the pipe diameter
  • Valley flashing: open valley (exposed metal) or closed-cut (shingles cover it) — the method affects both appearance and longevity

If an estimate lists a single "flashing" line for $200 on a home with a chimney, two valleys, and four plumbing vents, it is not a complete estimate.

7. Ventilation: Ridge Vent, Off-Ridge, and Soffit

Florida's heat makes attic ventilation a performance and code issue, not just a comfort one. Inadequate ventilation drives up cooling costs, accelerates shingle degradation, and can void manufacturer warranties on GAF, CertainTeed, and Owens Corning products. The estimate should specify:

  • Linear footage of ridge vent (continuous preferred over box vents)
  • Number of off-ridge vents, if applicable
  • Soffit vent condition and whether existing soffit ventilation is being cleared or repaired

Warranties, Permit Fee, and Labor

8. Manufacturer Warranty Tier

Shingle warranties vary by product tier. On a roof replacement, the estimate should identify:

  • Shingle product line (e.g., GAF Timberline HDZ, CertainTeed Landmark Pro, Owens Corning Duration)
  • Warranty duration: 25-year, Lifetime, or enhanced warranty requiring certified contractor installation
  • Whether the warranty is registered: certification-based warranties like GAF's Silver Pledge or Golden Pledge require contractor registration. If the contractor is not a GAF Master Elite contractor, those tiers are unavailable.

9. Labor Warranty

Separate from the manufacturer warranty is the contractor's labor warranty. This covers installation defects — flashing that lifts, a valley that leaks at year two, a pipe boot that was not seated properly. In Jacksonville, a credible labor warranty runs two to five years minimum. The estimate should state:

  • Duration
  • Named contact (not "the company" — a named principal)
  • What is and is not covered

10. Permit Fee

Roofing permits are required by Jacksonville code for full replacements and most re-roofs. The permit fee should appear as a separate line item in every estimate — typically $150–$400 in Duval County depending on project valuation. If it is not listed, ask directly. Some contractors pull permits under homeowner exemptions to cut costs — that shifts liability to you. Verify the contractor is pulling and paying for the permit themselves.

Red Flags: Estimates That Should Make You Walk Away

Even experienced homeowners comparing three bids may not spot every warning sign. These are the most common:

  • Single-number bid with no line items: no basis for comparison, no recourse if materials are substituted
  • No permit line: the job may be planned to run without a permit, creating a code violation on your property record
  • Flashing listed as a single $150–$250 line: physically impossible to flash a chimney, two valleys, and six pipe boots for that amount
  • Decking "as needed" with no per-unit price: blank check exposure
  • No ventilation line: common on low-bid jobs; the crew installs new shingles without assessing or correcting ventilation, which voids manufacturer warranties
  • No manufacturer warranty tier specified: you cannot verify what was installed after the job
  • No labor warranty duration: "we stand behind our work" is not a warranty
