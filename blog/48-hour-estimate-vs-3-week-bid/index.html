What Does a 48-Hour Estimate Tell You That a 3-Week Bid Process Doesn't?

May 4, 2026 · 6 min read · JK Prestige Constructor Corp

---

When you ask two Jacksonville general contractors (GCs) for an estimate, one comes back in 48 hours and one comes back in 3 weeks. The instinct is to assume the 3-week response is more thorough. That instinct is usually wrong.

The time it takes a GC to produce an estimate reveals their operating model — who does the work, where the numbers come from, and how much of the project they actually control. Understanding the difference helps you make a better contractor selection before you're locked into a contract.

---

Why a 3-Week Estimate Usually Means a Brokered Operation

A GC who takes 3 weeks to produce an estimate is typically doing this: sending your project scope to subcontractors, waiting for sub bids to return, marking those bids up, and assembling a proposal from other people's numbers.

This is not inherently wrong — most large commercial GCs operate this way. But it has specific implications:

The estimate quality depends on sub participation. If two of the three MEP (Mechanical, Electrical, and Plumbing) subcontractors the GC contacted didn't bid — which is common in a busy Jacksonville market — the GC is assembling a price with one or two competitive bids instead of three. The number you receive reflects the subs who chose to respond, not necessarily the market rate.

The GC doesn't know their own costs for the critical trades. If you have a question about why framing is priced at a specific number, the honest answer is: "because that's what the framing sub bid." There's no internal knowledge base to draw from. The estimate is as transparent as the sub it came from.

Resequencing is slow. If the project scope changes before contract signing, the GC has to go back to subs for revised numbers. More time, more rounds, more waiting.

The GC becomes a coordinator, not a builder. Once the project starts, that same dynamic plays out on site: the GC is scheduling and coordinating subs they don't control. Schedule delays and quality issues from subs become the owner's problem to absorb.

---

What a 48-Hour Estimate Actually Requires

A GC who can produce a complete, line-item estimate within 48 hours of a site walk has one of two things: deep domain knowledge of their own cost structure, or a very short and underdefined scope document.

You can tell the difference by looking at what the estimate contains.

A 48-hour estimate that is complete includes:

  • Line-item scope breakdown by trade
  • Per-trade pricing (framing, concrete, drywall, MEP coordination, roofing, finish carpentry)
  • Material specifications referenced (not allowances)
  • Payment schedule tied to milestones
  • Warranty terms
  • Defined exclusions

A 48-hour estimate that is vague — a single total number with no breakdown, or a total with multiple large allowances — is not evidence of speed. It's evidence of a short document. The real cost negotiations happen later, through change orders.

How to Use the 48-Hour Standard to Compare GCs

When you're evaluating multiple GCs, request a 48-hour turnaround from all of them. The results are informative:

The GC who can't deliver in 48 hours is either brokering subs, understaffed, or not prioritizing your project. Ask them directly: what's in your estimate that takes 3 weeks to produce? Listen carefully to the answer.

The GC who delivers in 48 hours with a vague document is using speed to generate a low-looking number. They'll fill the gap with change orders. Compare the detail in the estimate — specifically how many allowances versus fixed prices appear — not just the total.

The GC who delivers in 48 hours with a complete line-item document is showing you their operating model. They know their costs, they control their trades, and they're prepared to lock a fixed price. This is the document you want.

Take both estimates and put them side by side on a per-line basis. See which lines have fixed prices and which have allowances. See which exclusions are clearly listed. See whose payment schedule favors the owner (milestone-based) versus the GC (front-loaded calendar payments). The comparison will tell you more than the total number does.
