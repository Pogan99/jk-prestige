Hurricane Season Roof Prep for Jacksonville Homeowners: What to Inspect Before June 1

May 4, 2026 · 7 min read · JK Prestige Constructor Corp

Hurricane season runs June 1 through November 30. Jacksonville is in the direct path of Atlantic storms tracking up the Florida coast, and every year there are homeowners who discover a roof problem at 11pm during a named storm — a problem a $400 repair in April would have fixed.

The window to schedule a roof inspection and complete pre-season repairs is March through May. By late May, roofing crews across Jacksonville are booked weeks out as storm anxiety peaks. By June, you're waiting.

Here's what to inspect, what to look for, and what to do if you find a problem.

---

Why Pre-Hurricane Roof Prep Matters More in Jacksonville

Jacksonville's position in northeast Florida means it takes hits from two storm tracks: Gulf storms that cross the Florida peninsula and continue northeast, and Atlantic storms that make landfall on the First Coast or pass offshore with significant wind and rain bands. The city also sits within a coastal high-wind zone — Duval County's design wind speed for residential construction is 130 mph under ASCE 7-22.

A roof that's 80% intact is not a roof that performs at 80% in a 100 mph sustained wind event. Wind uplift is not linear — a roof with five missing shingles has a significantly higher failure probability than a fully intact roof because the exposed deck creates a pressure differential that accelerates uplift on adjacent shingles. Pre-season repairs are not cosmetic — they are structural.

---

7-Point Pre-Hurricane Roof Inspection Checklist

1. Field Shingles: Lifting, Curling, and Granule Loss

Walk the perimeter of your home with binoculars and scan each roof plane from ground level. You're looking for:

  • Lifting shingles: the seal tab (the factory-applied adhesive strip) breaks down with age and UV exposure. When the seal fails, wind can get under the shingle leading edge and start the peel. Look for shingle edges that appear raised or wavy.
  • Curling: two failure modes. Cupping (edges curl upward) is moisture-related and indicates the shingle is drying unevenly. Clawing (middle rises while edges stay flat) indicates age and brittleness.
  • Granule loss: Florida's UV and heat cycle accelerate granule erosion. Minor granule loss distributed evenly is normal aging. Significant bare patches on the mat surface indicate the shingle is near end of life.

Do not get on the roof yourself for this initial assessment. Binoculars from the ground give you enough information to know whether a professional inspection is warranted.

2. Ridge and Hip Caps: First to Fail in Wind

Ridge caps and hip caps are the highest-exposed shingles on any roof and the ones most vulnerable to wind uplift. They receive full wind exposure on both sides, their seal tabs are often the first to degrade from UV exposure, and a failed ridge cap exposes the underlying deck to water infiltration at the peak of the roof — the worst possible location for a leak.

Inspect ridge caps specifically. Missing, lifted, or cracked ridge caps in April should be repaired before June 1 without hesitation.

3. Step and Counter Flashing at Chimneys and Dormers

Flashings — the metal transitions between the roof and vertical surfaces — are the most common source of roof leaks in Jacksonville homes. Two flashing types are critical at chimneys and dormers:

  • Step flashing: individual L-shaped pieces of metal interwoven with shingles along a sloped wall. They should be flat and tight. Lifted or corroded step flashing creates a water entry point at every course.
  • Counter flashing: the metal piece embedded in the mortar joint of a chimney or set into a dormer wall, covering the top of the step flashing. Cracked mortar joints or separated counter flashing are common after years of thermal cycling in Jacksonville's heat.

These are not visible from the ground. A professional inspection is required to assess flashing condition accurately.

4. Pipe Boot Flashings: Rubber Cracks in Florida UV

Plumbing vent stacks penetrate the roof deck and are sealed with a pipe boot flashing — a metal base with a rubber collar that fits around the pipe. The rubber degrades in Florida's UV and heat. A cracked pipe boot is an active leak point every time it rains.

Pipe boots typically last 7-10 years in Florida's climate. On a 15-year-old roof, every pipe boot is a candidate for replacement. This is a $75-$150 repair per pipe — and it's one of the simplest ways to prevent a water damage claim.

5. Drip Edge: Secured or Susceptible to Wind?

Drip edge is the metal flashing installed at the eave and rake edges of the roof. Florida Building Code requires drip edge to be mechanically fastened — nailed, not just laid in place. Loose or unsecured drip edge lifts in wind and creates a peeling entry point that can propagate across the eave.

On older roofs, drip edge was sometimes installed without adequate fastening, or the fastening has corroded. Check the drip edge at the eave corners — you can often see whether the edge is laying flat and tight or has begun to lift.

6. Gutters and Downspouts: Debris Creates Backup

Clogged gutters are a roofing issue, not just a drainage issue. When gutters fill with debris and back up during heavy rain, water pools at the eave and can back up under the drip edge and shingles. In a Jacksonville tropical rain event — 3-4 inches in 90 minutes is not unusual — a partially clogged gutter creates the conditions for an interior water intrusion event.

Before hurricane season: clean gutters completely, verify downspout discharge is clear and directing water at least 4 feet from the foundation, and confirm that gutters are securely attached to the fascia. Gutter spikes pull out over time — gutter screws are more reliable in wind.

7. Attic Ventilation: Pressure Differential in Storm Conditions

Attic ventilation affects roof performance in wind events through pressure physics. A well-ventilated attic with clear soffit vents and a ridge vent maintains balanced pressure. A blocked attic — clogged soffit vents, insufficient net free area — creates a positive pressure differential in storm conditions that adds to the uplift force on the roof deck.

From inside the attic (when it's safe and cool to access): look for blocked soffit vents (often covered by insulation that was installed over the vent openings), soffit vent screens that are intact, and any sign of moisture intrusion at the deck.

---

When to Schedule: The March Through May Window

If you schedule your roof inspection in March, you have the most flexibility. Any repair identified — whether it's a pipe boot replacement, step flashing repair, or partial shingle replacement — can be completed in 2-3 weeks with no urgency premium.

By late April, roofing crews begin to fill up. By mid-May, scheduling a repair often means a 3-4 week wait. By June 1, you're in storm season and the calculus changes completely.
