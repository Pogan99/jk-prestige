Whole-House Renovation vs. Home Addition in Jacksonville: How to Choose

June 6, 2026 · 7 min read · JK Prestige Constructor Corp

Most Jacksonville homeowners who call JK asking about adding a bedroom or expanding a kitchen are actually facing a broader decision: do we renovate the existing space, do we add on to the house, or do we do both? The answer is not the same for every house, every neighborhood, or every budget. Here is the framework JK uses to help homeowners think through it.

What You Are Actually Trying to Solve

Before the conversation turns to square footage and cost, start with the problem statement:

  • Is the house too small, or is the existing space poorly configured?
  • Are you missing a specific room, or is the layout of existing rooms the frustration?
  • Are you fixing outdated finishes, or do you need structural changes?

A 2,200 sqft house with three bedrooms and two bathrooms where the kitchen is awkwardly separated from the living area is a layout problem. A gut renovation that reconfigures the floor plan may solve it completely without adding a single square foot.

A 2,200 sqft house with three bedrooms and two bathrooms that needs a fourth bedroom for a growing family is a square footage problem. No amount of renovation solves a bedroom count problem — an addition is the path.

The first question is always: what specifically is the house failing to provide?

When Renovation Is the Better Answer

Choose whole-house renovation over addition when:

  • The layout is the problem, not the size — reconfiguring walls, opening up kitchen-to-living, expanding a cramped primary bath within the existing footprint often delivers more livability than square footage alone
  • The finishes are the problem — outdated kitchens, original bathrooms, and worn flooring do not require an addition to fix; a gut renovation of the existing space is typically the answer
  • The lot won't support an addition — setbacks, existing outbuildings, mature trees, or irregular lot shapes sometimes make addition impossible or so expensive it makes no sense
  • You are in a neighborhood where lot size limits addition value — in some Jacksonville neighborhoods, adding 600 sqft to a 1,400 sqft house on a 6,000 sqft lot produces a house that is priced above what the street supports; renovation ROI is better

Whole-house gut renovations in Jacksonville — new kitchen, all bathrooms, flooring, paint, trim, electrical panel upgrade, HVAC replacement — typically cost $90-150/sqft of existing living area. On a 1,800 sqft house, that is $160,000-270,000 for a top-to-bottom transformation.

JK self-performs the high-impact interior trades on renovation: framing (for wall reconfiguration), drywall, and finish carpentry. The quality of the finish on a renovation shows in the trim, the door casings, the built-in shelving, and the custom millwork — the items that differentiate a JK renovation from a contractor who farms everything out.

When Addition Is the Better Answer

Choose addition over renovation when:

  • You need a specific room type you simply do not have — fourth bedroom, home office with separate entrance, in-law suite, formal dining room
  • The primary suite is undersized and cannot be reconfigured — some houses have structural constraints that prevent expanding the primary suite within the existing footprint; the only path is extending out
  • You want to add an ADU or income-producing unit — Jacksonville and Duval County increasingly allow accessory dwelling units; a detached addition with its own entrance and kitchenette is a different product than interior renovation
  • The lot supports it without variance — if there is buildable area within setbacks and the footprint makes sense, addition is often the cleanest path

Home additions in Jacksonville run $150-250/sqft for one-story construction including foundation, framing, MEP, drywall, and finishes. A 500 sqft primary suite addition — bedroom, bathroom, walk-in closet — typically costs $100,000-180,000 depending on finish level.

The "Both" Option: Renovation Plus Addition

Many Jacksonville homeowners end up doing both — adding space in one direction while reconfiguring and updating the existing interior. This is usually the highest-ROI path when:

  • The existing house needs updates regardless
  • There is a clear functional need the addition addresses
  • Doing both together is cheaper than two separate projects (mobilization costs, temporary barrier work, permit fees, and design costs are more efficient combined)

A renovation-plus-addition scope on a 1,600 sqft house — gut kitchen and bathrooms, open floor plan, add 400 sqft primary suite — typically runs $250,000-400,000 depending on finish level and site conditions. That is a significant number, but it delivers a complete transformation rather than a partial improvement.

Permit Differences: Renovation vs. Addition

Both require building permits in Duval County, but the complexity differs:

  • Interior renovation (no structural changes): Building permit with architectural drawings; typically 2-4 weeks review
  • Interior renovation with structural changes (wall removal, new openings): Requires structural drawings stamped by PE; 3-6 weeks review
  • Home addition: Building permit with structural drawings, site plan showing addition footprint, and energy compliance; 4-8 weeks review

For either path, JK manages the permit process. The permit fee is included in the fixed-price contract. Waiting on the permit is baked into the project timeline — not charged as extra time.

The Setback Check: Do Before You Design

If you are considering an addition, the first step before spending money on design is to determine whether your lot can accommodate it. Measure from your house to each property line and check your zoning district's setback requirements:

  • Find your zoning designation on the City of Jacksonville's property search
  • Look up required setbacks for your zone in the Land Development Code
  • Subtract setbacks from property line distances
  • Confirm the remaining buildable area accommodates the addition you want

If the lot cannot accommodate the addition without a variance, the variance process costs time and money and is not guaranteed approval. Know this before commissioning architectural drawings.

ROI Comparison: Jacksonville Market

Renovation ROI in Jacksonville depends heavily on neighborhood:

  • In Springfield, Riverside, Avondale, and San Marco, high-quality renovation typically returns $0.80-1.10 for every dollar spent on resale
  • In suburban Jacksonville neighborhoods (Mandarin, Baymeadows, Southside), renovation ROI tends to be $0.60-0.90 on the dollar

Addition ROI is similarly neighborhood-dependent:

  • Adding a primary suite or fourth bedroom in a neighborhood where comparable homes have that configuration adds value at or above cost
  • Adding a fifth bedroom to a street of three-bedroom homes delivers less ROI — the neighborhood ceiling constrains it

This is not an argument against renovation or addition for quality-of-life reasons. But if you are expecting dollar-for-dollar ROI, understand the neighborhood ceiling first.
