5 Safety Certifications to Look for in a Florida GC

May 4, 2026 · 6 min read · JK Prestige Constructor Corp

Florida's construction industry employs more than 650,000 workers statewide, and the state consistently ranks among the top five in the country for construction fatalities per year. The Florida Division of Safety — operating under the Department of Financial Services — enforces worker safety standards alongside federal OSHA, but enforcement is reactive. Nobody shows up to verify a crew's safety credentials before a project starts. That verification is on the owner.

Asking a GC about safety certifications is not a bureaucratic exercise. A contractor who can document their certifications is a contractor who has invested in their workforce. A contractor who deflects the question is telling you something. The five items below are specific, verifiable, and meaningful — not just checkbox credentials.

1. OSHA 30 for All Supervisors

The OSHA 30-hour Construction Safety and Health course is the standard training credential for field supervisors — superintendents, foremen, and lead carpenters who direct the work of others. The 30-hour course covers fall protection, electrical hazards, struck-by hazards, caught-in-between hazards, and a range of topic areas specific to construction site conditions. Completion generates a plastic wallet card issued by OSHA's Training Institute. Ask to see the card for the superintendent who will run your project. OSHA 30 is not a license — OSHA does not maintain a public lookup — but the card itself is issued with the cardholder's name and completion date, which you can verify on request. JK Prestige Construction's OSHA 30 crews hold this credential at the supervisor level on every project.

2. OSHA 10 for All Field Workers

OSHA 10 is the entry-level, 10-hour construction safety course required for all workers on many federally funded and institutionally regulated projects. While not universally mandated on private residential work in Florida, it signals that a GC has established a baseline of safety training across their field labor force — not just their supervisors. A crew where every worker holds OSHA 10 is a crew that understands hazard identification, the right to refuse unsafe work, and site-specific safety planning. Ask a GC what percentage of their field workers hold current OSHA 10 cards. A company that self-performs multiple trades — framing, concrete, drywall — should be able to answer that question for each trade crew.

3. First Aid and CPR for Superintendent and Foreman

OSHA standard 29 CFR 1926.50 requires that at least one person at each construction worksite be trained in first aid and CPR, with access to a first aid kit at all times. The requirement is for the person to be "adequately trained" — the practical standard is current certification from the American Red Cross or American Heart Association, renewed on the organization's published schedule (typically two years for CPR). A superintendent or foreman who holds a current First Aid/CPR card is documented as the designated emergency responder for that site. In Jacksonville, where the nearest trauma center from many active construction sites in outlying areas like Nocatee or Amelia Island may be 20 to 30 minutes away, having a trained responder on site is operationally significant, not merely procedural.

4. Competent Person Designation

OSHA uses the term "competent person" to identify the field-designated individual responsible for specific high-hazard work activities. Competent person designation is required — and documented separately — for trenching and excavation, scaffolding, fall protection systems, concrete formwork, and several other operations. The designation is not a card or a certificate: it is a formal assignment from the company, combined with demonstrated training and authority to stop work for safety violations. Ask your GC to name the competent person for excavation and for fall protection, and to explain that person's authority to remove workers from a hazardous situation. A GC who cannot answer this question does not have a documented safety management system — they are relying on crew experience alone.

5. TRIR and EMR Records — Ask for Three Years

Two numbers tell you more about a GC's safety culture than any certificate: the Total Recordable Incident Rate (TRIR) and the Experience Modification Rate (EMR). TRIR is calculated from OSHA 300 logs — the mandatory record of all workplace injuries requiring medical treatment beyond first aid. TRIR = (number of incidents x 200,000) / total hours worked. An industry-standard TRIR for general contractors is approximately 3.0; institutional-quality GCs maintain TRIRs below 1.0. EMR is issued by the company's workers' compensation insurer and reflects three years of claim history relative to industry average. An EMR of 1.0 is average; below 1.0 is better-than-average. Request both the three-year OSHA 300 logs and the current EMR modifier from any GC you are seriously considering. A GC unwilling to share this data is a GC with something to hide.

FAQ

Is OSHA 30 required by law in Florida for general contractors?

OSHA 30 is not universally required by Florida state law for all GCs. However, it is required on many public projects, school construction projects, and projects with institutional owners. Regardless of legal requirement, it is a strong proxy for the quality of a GC's safety management program.

Where can I look up a Florida contractor's license and complaint history?

The Florida Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR) maintains a public online lookup at myfloridalicense.com where you can search by license number or company name and see the license status, expiration, and any administrative complaints or disciplinary actions.

What is the difference between a Duval County construction permit and OSHA compliance?

They are separate systems. A building permit is issued by Duval County Building Services and governs code compliance — structure, fire safety, mechanical, electrical, and plumbing. OSHA compliance is a federal worker safety standard that governs how the work is performed, not what is built. A permit does not verify that a crew holds OSHA 10, and an OSHA 10 card does not satisfy any building code requirement.

Can I request a contractor's OSHA 300 logs before signing?

Yes. OSHA 300 logs are internal documents, not public records, but a GC can voluntarily share them with a prospective client. Requesting them is standard practice in commercial construction and increasingly common in custom residential work. A three-year summary is sufficient — you are looking for trend direction, not individual incident details.
