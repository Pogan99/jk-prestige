---

Why Most Jacksonville Contractors Take Weeks to Follow Up

The delay is structural, not personal. Most general contractors operate by brokering work out to subcontractors. When a homeowner calls about a kitchen renovation or an addition, the GC's first step is to send scope sheets to three or four subcontractors for each trade — framing, drywall, electrical, plumbing, tile — and wait for bids to come back. The GC then assembles those bids, adds markup, and produces an estimate.

That process takes two to four weeks under normal conditions. When any one subcontractor is slow to respond, it takes longer. When the GC is running several bids simultaneously across other projects, it takes longer still.

The other factor is the site walk itself. At many contractors, the site walk is conducted by a salesperson or an estimator — not the person who will actually manage your project. The salesperson documents the scope and hands it off to the estimating team, adding another handoff and another delay.

None of that is dishonest. It is just how a brokered-out business works.

The JK Prestige 48-Hour Estimate Process

JK Prestige's 48-hour turnaround is possible because of two structural differences: self-perform trades and principal-led site walks.

Step 1: Site Walk Scheduled Within 24 Hours

When a homeowner contacts JK Prestige, a site walk is scheduled within 24 hours. Not two weeks out — 24 hours. The site walk is conducted by a JK Prestige principal, not a salesperson or an estimating coordinator.

Step 2: The Principal Conducts the Walk

The principal who runs the site walk is the same person who will sign your contract and manage your project. This is not a sales call. The walk has a specific purpose: to lock the scope.

During the walk, the principal documents:

  • Existing conditions (structural, mechanical, finish conditions that affect scope)
  • Work included in the quote (line by line — what trades, what materials, what finishes)
  • Work explicitly excluded (what the homeowner is responsible for, or what is in an allowance)
  • Any conditions that could change the scope (existing structural issues, hidden plumbing, permit requirements)

The scope is locked during the walk. The principal does not return to the office and ask subcontractors for bids. JK Prestige self-performs framing, concrete, drywall, finish carpentry, and roofing — the trades that drive most of the cost variability in a residential renovation or custom home project. Because JK knows its own crew costs and material pricing, the estimate can be built from the walk notes, not from pending subcontractor bids.

Step 3: Written Fixed-Price Estimate Delivered in 48 Hours

The estimate delivered within 48 hours of the site walk includes:

  • Line-item scope: Each trade is broken out. What framing work, what drywall scope, what finish carpentry. Not a single lump-sum number with no backup.
  • Fixed price: If the scope documented in the walk does not change, the price does not change. No change-order theater mid-project.
  • Payment schedule: Milestone-based payments tied to documented construction progress, not arbitrary draw dates.
  • Warranty terms: Year-one workmanship warranty with the principal's name on it, not a corporate warranty department.

The estimate is a document you can take to your lender, your architect, or your spouse. It is specific enough to be actionable and complete enough to be a contract.

What the 48-Hour Estimate Includes — and What It Assumes

Being clear about the boundaries of the estimate is part of how the 48-hour commitment works. The estimate covers the scope documented during the walk. It does not cover:

  • Hidden conditions discovered after demolition (rot behind walls, existing plumbing not to code, undersized electrical panels). These are documented as potential scope items in the estimate with a note that they will be priced separately if found. They are not buried in the fixed price as a contingency that inflates the original number.
  • Owner-requested scope changes after the contract is signed. If you add a mudroom after framing starts, that is a scope change and it gets a written change order with a price before any work is added. Fixed-price means fixed-scope — not fixed-everything-including-things-we-haven't-decided-yet.
  • Allowances for owner-selected items. Flooring, tile, plumbing fixtures, and light fixtures are often specified as allowances — a budgeted dollar amount per square foot or per unit. If the homeowner selects materials above the allowance, the difference is added. Below the allowance, it comes back.

Explaining this during the site walk is part of the principal's job. A homeowner who understands the estimate structure is a homeowner who won't be surprised by the first change order.

What to Do With the Estimate When You Receive It

A 48-hour written estimate from JK Prestige is a complete document. To evaluate it against other contractors:

  1. Compare scope, not price. A lower number from another contractor that doesn't include the same trades or finish level is not a lower price — it is an incomplete scope. Ask each contractor to match scope items line for line before comparing totals.
  1. Confirm who self-performs. If another contractor's lower price is based on brokered-out framing and drywall, the quality control, schedule risk, and change-order exposure are different. Ask specifically: do your own crews do the framing? The drywall?
  1. Ask about the payment schedule. Front-loaded payment schedules (large deposits before work begins) are a warning sign. JK Prestige's payment schedule is milestone-based.
  1. Check the warranty language. "One-year warranty" can mean many things. Ask who the warranty contact is, what the response time commitment is, and whether the principal handles warranty calls or a separate department.
