What Is a Fixed-Price Construction Contract? How It Differs From Cost-Plus and GMP

May 4, 2026 · 7 min read · JK Prestige Constructor Corp

---

Before a GC breaks ground, you sign a contract. That contract determines who absorbs cost overruns, how you'll be billed, what visibility you have into where the money goes, and how disputes get resolved. Most owners don't understand the contract type they're signing until there's a problem.

Three contract types dominate construction: fixed-price, cost-plus, and GMP (Guaranteed Maximum Price). Each one is appropriate for different project conditions. Each one places risk differently. And each one has a version that works for owners and a version that doesn't.

---

Fixed-Price Contracts: You Pay X, Period

A fixed-price contract — sometimes called a lump-sum contract — is exactly what it sounds like. The GC and owner agree on a price before work begins, and that price doesn't change unless the scope changes.

If the GC encounters higher lumber costs, a less efficient crew, or longer-than-expected concrete cure times, those are the GC's problem. The price stays fixed. The owner pays the agreed number.

What fixed-price requires to work:

Fixed-price only works when the scope is completely defined before the contract is signed. That means:

  • Full architectural drawings completed and approved
  • All structural engineering complete
  • All material selections made (fixtures, finishes, appliances, flooring)
  • Site conditions understood (soil tests done, underground utilities located)
  • All permit applications submitted or approvable

If any of these elements are incomplete, the GC will either build contingency into the price (you pay for it indirectly) or they'll issue change orders when the unknowns surface (you pay for it later). Neither outcome serves the owner.

When fixed-price is appropriate:

Fixed-price is the right contract type for residential construction (custom homes and gut-renos) where full selections can be made before ground breaks, and for commercial projects with fully completed design documents. It's also appropriate for smaller commercial fit-outs where the scope is well-defined.

JK Prestige uses fixed-price contracts for residential projects. The price doesn't change if the scope doesn't change. That's the commitment the owner gets in writing before we spend a day on site.

What to watch out for:

A GC who offers a fixed price before the design is complete is either hiding contingency in the number or planning to recover it through change orders. Fixed-price requires full scope documentation — not a preliminary design and a ballpark.

---

Cost-Plus Contracts: You Pay Actual Costs Plus a Fee

A cost-plus contract means the owner pays the actual cost of all labor and materials, plus a GC fee — either a fixed dollar amount or a percentage of costs (typically 10-15%).

There is no ceiling. Every cost increase goes directly to the owner. If lumber prices spike, you pay more. If the GC's crew takes longer than planned, you pay more. The GC's profit is protected (they earn their fee regardless), but the owner's exposure is unlimited.

When cost-plus is appropriate:

Cost-plus is appropriate for projects where the scope cannot be defined in advance — complex historic renovations with unknown structural conditions, projects that will evolve significantly during construction, or fast-track work where design and construction overlap. In these situations, forcing a GC to commit to a fixed price means they'll pad it heavily or fight every change order.

Cost-plus also works when the owner has unlimited trust in the GC's cost management and a transparent relationship with the GC's accounting. In practice, this is rare.

What to watch out for:

Cost-plus without a ceiling or audit rights is the highest-risk contract type for an owner. GC fees structured as a percentage of costs give the GC a financial incentive to let costs run high — their fee increases as the project gets more expensive. If you're considering cost-plus, require: (1) a GC fee structured as a fixed dollar amount, not a percentage; (2) owner audit rights for all invoices and receipts; (3) a cost estimate that functions as a target, even if it's not guaranteed.

---

GMP Contracts: Open Books With a Ceiling

A Guaranteed Maximum Price (GMP) contract is a hybrid. The owner pays actual costs — like cost-plus — but the GC guarantees the total won't exceed a specified maximum.

The GMP is the ceiling. Costs that go over the GMP are the GC's problem. Costs that come in under the GMP may be shared between the GC and owner through a savings clause (for example, 50% of savings go to the owner, 50% go to the GC as an incentive for efficient management).

How a GMP contract is structured:

The GMP contract includes several key components:

  • GMP amount: The ceiling — the maximum the owner will pay
  • GC fee: A percentage of actual costs, typically 3-8% on commercial projects; this is the GC's profit
  • General conditions: Superintendent, safety program, project management, temporary facilities — these should be a fixed line item, not a percentage
  • Contingency: A pool of reserve funds for unforeseen conditions; properly structured, there are two separate contingencies: one the GC controls, one the owner controls
  • Open-book costs: Every subcontractor invoice and material receipt is submitted to the owner for review — there are no hidden costs

When GMP is appropriate:

GMP is the standard contract type for large commercial and healthcare construction — projects over $5M where the design may still be evolving at contract signing, or where the owner needs budget certainty but also wants cost transparency.

JK Prestige uses GMP contracts for large commercial and hospital projects. The open-book structure means the owner sees every dollar spent. The GMP ceiling means the owner's budget exposure is defined.

What to watch out for:

A GMP without a savings-sharing clause means the GC keeps every dollar of savings — you get cost certainty, but no upside if the project runs efficiently. Also watch for general conditions structured as a percentage of costs (they should be fixed), and a single-contingency pool that the GC can draw from without owner approval.

Which Contract Type Protects You

No contract type automatically protects an owner. What protects an owner is a GC who operates transparently, produces a full scope document before execution, and doesn't use the contract structure to hide cost exposure.

For residential projects: fixed-price with a full scope document before ground breaks. You should know exactly what you're getting before you sign.

For large commercial: GMP with open-book costs, a fixed general conditions line, separate contingencies, and a savings-sharing clause.

The contract structure matters — but the GC's behavior within that structure matters more. A cost-plus contract with a GC who is genuinely transparent produces better outcomes than a fixed-price contract with a GC who hides scope gaps and then fires change orders.
