What Is a Notice of Commencement in Florida and Why It Must Be Filed Before Construction Starts

May 4, 2026 · 6 min read · JK Prestige Constructor Corp

---

Before any permitted construction project begins in Florida on a property improvement valued at more than $2,500, one document must be filed, recorded, and posted at the jobsite: the Notice of Commencement (NOC). This is not bureaucratic paperwork. It is the legal foundation of your protection under Florida's construction lien law — and skipping it or filing it incorrectly can leave you paying for work twice.

This post explains what a Notice of Commencement is, why Florida law requires it, who is responsible for filing it, and what JK Prestige does with it as part of every pre-construction process.

---

What Is a Notice of Commencement?

A Notice of Commencement is a legal document that establishes a public record of a construction project on a specific property. It identifies:

  • The property owner
  • The property being improved (legal description and parcel ID)
  • The general contractor (GC) of record
  • The project description and location
  • The date construction will begin
  • The date the NOC expires (typically one year from recording)
  • The surety bonding information (if a payment bond is in place)
  • The lender's information (if the project is financed)

The NOC is recorded with the county clerk — in Jacksonville, that's the Duval County Clerk of Court — and must be physically posted at the jobsite before work begins. A certified copy of the recorded NOC is what gets posted.

---

Why Florida Law Requires a Notice of Commencement

Florida's Construction Lien Law (Chapter 713, Florida Statutes) gives contractors, subcontractors, and material suppliers the right to place a claim of lien against real property if they aren't paid for work performed or materials furnished. The lien right is powerful: it attaches to the property itself, not just to the GC who hired the sub.

This creates a risk that Florida property owners often don't understand until they experience it: you can pay your GC in full and still have a lien placed on your property by a subcontractor the GC didn't pay. The framing sub, the concrete supplier, the drywall contractor — if any of these parties deliver work or materials to your project and don't get paid by the GC, they have the right to lien your property under Chapter 713.

The Notice of Commencement is the mechanism that organizes this system and creates the owner's protection.

---

How the NOC Protects the Owner

When a Notice of Commencement is properly filed and posted, it triggers several protections:

Preliminary Notice obligation. Under Florida law, subcontractors and suppliers who want to preserve their lien rights must serve a "Notice to Owner" on the property owner within 45 days of first furnishing labor, materials, or services. This Notice to Owner alerts the property owner that a sub or supplier is on the project and may claim a lien if not paid.

The owner's visibility into the lien chain. When you receive a Notice to Owner from a framing sub you've never heard of, you know three things: that sub is on your project, they've delivered services, and they have active lien rights. This gives you the information to verify — with the GC — that the sub is being paid before you release the next payment to the GC.

Lien waiver coordination. With the NOC establishing the lien chain, the owner can require the GC to provide lien waivers from major subs and suppliers with each draw. A lien waiver is a signed statement from the sub or supplier confirming they've been paid to date and releasing their lien rights for that amount.

Protection against double payment claims. If an NOC is missing and a sub properly serves a Notice to Owner but the owner never received it (because there was no NOC to direct the notice process), the lien system is murkier and the owner's defenses are weaker. A properly filed NOC creates the record that makes the entire system function as designed.

---

What Happens Without a Notice of Commencement

You may pay twice. If the GC doesn't pay a major subcontractor and that sub records a claim of lien against your property, you have limited defenses if the NOC process wasn't followed correctly. The sub's lien may be valid and enforceable. Satisfying the lien requires either paying the sub directly (which means paying for work you already paid the GC for), bonding off the lien, or litigating it.

Permits may not be issued. City of Jacksonville Building Inspection Division requires a recorded NOC before issuing certain building permits. The permit won't issue, and construction can't legally begin, without the NOC in place.

The title is clouded. Unresolved construction liens appear on a title search. If you plan to sell the property, refinance, or obtain a construction-to-permanent loan, liens and NOC deficiencies will appear at title review and can block the transaction.

---

Who Files the Notice of Commencement?

Under Florida Statute 713.13, the property owner files the Notice of Commencement — it's the owner's legal document. However, in practice, the GC typically prepares and files the NOC on the owner's behalf as part of the pre-construction process.

This is standard practice and legally permissible. The GC prepares the NOC, the owner signs it (or authorizes the GC to sign as agent), and the GC records it with the Duval County Clerk of Court before any work begins.

What the owner should verify:

  • The NOC accurately identifies the property (legal description matches the parcel)
  • The GC named is the GC who signed your contract
  • The project description matches your permitted scope
  • The effective date is before the first day of construction
  • A certified copy is posted at the jobsite before work begins

Recording cost: In Duval County, NOC recording fees are based on document length — typically $10-$30. This is a project cost the GC passes through to the owner.
