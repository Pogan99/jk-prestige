Fixed-Price Custom Home Contracts: How to Protect Yourself From Mid-Build Budget Drift

May 4, 2026 · 8 min read · JK Prestige Constructor Corp

A fixed-price custom home contract sounds like protection. You sign, a number is written on the page, and that number is what you pay. In practice, many fixed-price contracts are not fixed at all — they are fixed-price envelopes containing unfixed components called allowances, and the gap between the allowance and the actual cost is where budget drift lives.

Budget drift in a Florida custom home build is not a contractor ethics issue. It is a scope precision issue. A GC who sets allowances based on what they can fit into your budget, rather than what the market actually charges for the materials you want, will produce a contract number that reads as fixed but will grow the moment you select a specific product. Understanding how a real fixed-price contract works — and what it requires from both parties before ground breaks — is the most effective protection a homeowner has.

What Allowances Are and Why They Create Budget Drift

An allowance is a budget placeholder in a construction contract for a material or finish that has not yet been selected. The contract is executed with an allowance amount that represents the GC's estimate of what the homeowner will spend on that item. When the homeowner selects the actual product, the contract adjusts up or down based on the difference between the allowance and the actual cost.

Allowances are a legitimate tool for materials that genuinely cannot be selected at contract time — unusual custom elements with long lead times that require final design confirmation before specifying. They become a problem when they are used as a way to reduce the contract number to make it more attractive to sign.

A GC who wants to win your project has an incentive to set allowances low. If the kitchen cabinetry allowance is $45,000 and you build a kitchen that costs $75,000 in cabinetry, you owe $30,000 above the contract number. If that pattern repeats across flooring, lighting fixtures, plumbing fixtures, and appliances — all commonly set as allowances — a contract that read as $1.6M at signing becomes $2.1M at completion. The project did not go over budget. The budget was never fully priced.

JK Prestige's position: allowances should be a last resort, not a standard contract structure. Every item that can be selected before ground breaks should be selected before ground breaks. For items that genuinely cannot be selected yet, JK uses market-rate allowances — priced to what the selection will likely cost, not what fits in the budget headline.

How Time-and-Materials Contracts Create a Different — And Worse — Risk

Some homeowners, after a bad experience with a fixed-price contract that drifted, conclude that time-and-materials (T&M) contracting is more transparent. It can be. It can also cost significantly more.

A T&M contract has no ceiling. The GC bills actual labor hours, actual material costs, and a markup percentage — typically 15–25% — on everything. There is no financial incentive for the GC to work efficiently. Schedule overruns do not cost the GC anything; they generate more billable hours. Material overorders are billed to the owner plus markup. Every change costs real money, but the owner has no way to forecast how much until the invoice arrives.

T&M works in situations where the scope genuinely cannot be defined in advance — a gut-reno with unknown wall conditions, a historic renovation with unpredictable subsurface findings. For a custom home on a surveyed lot with completed architectural plans, there is no reason to accept an open-ended billing structure.

The GC's operating model is what determines which contract type is appropriate, not the homeowner's preference. A GC who cannot give you a fixed price on a custom home with complete plans is telling you either that they do not know the market well enough to price it, or that they do not want to carry the risk of a defined scope. Both are informative.

What a Real Fixed-Price Contract Requires Before Ground Breaks

A fixed-price contract that will not drift requires four elements to be in place before the contract is signed and before ground breaks.

1. A complete scope document. Every room, every finish, every system, every material — specified in writing and attached to the contract. Not described in general terms ("high-end kitchen cabinetry") but in specific terms ("custom inset cabinetry by [manufacturer], as specified in cabinet design drawings dated [date], installed in the kitchen at approximately 47 linear feet of base cabinets and 32 linear feet of wall cabinets"). The scope document is the basis for the fixed price. Vague scope produces a price that cannot be defended when a homeowner makes a different selection than the GC assumed.

2. Material selections complete before ground breaks. Not before the frame is complete. Before ground breaks. The items that must be selected before construction begins: windows, exterior doors, roofing material, flooring by room, plumbing fixture schedule, appliance model numbers, and any custom millwork or cabinetry requiring shop drawings. These selections drive rough-in dimensions, structural support requirements, and procurement schedules. Selecting them after mobilization guarantees field changes.

3. Long-lead items identified and ordered. A fixed-price contract is not fixed if a long-lead item — a custom steel stair railing, a European window system, a specialty roofing material — is identified mid-construction and adds 10 weeks to the schedule. Long-lead items must be identified in pre-construction, ordered before ground breaks, and tracked against a delivery schedule tied to the construction schedule.

4. A defined subsurface contingency. Conditions below grade — soil bearing capacity, groundwater level, underground utilities, buried debris — cannot be guaranteed by any soil boring program. A real fixed-price contract defines what happens when subsurface conditions differ from the geotechnical report: what specific conditions trigger additional cost, and what the cost mechanism looks like (unit prices per cubic yard, defined allowance for alternative foundation systems). A contract that says "additional foundation work at actual cost" is an open-ended carve-out, not a contingency.

What Happens When Actual Conditions Differ From the Contract Assumptions

Even a well-structured fixed-price contract cannot anticipate every field condition. The question is how the contract handles those conditions when they arise.

Scope changes initiated by the owner are straightforward. A homeowner who decides mid-construction to add a bonus room, change a tile selection, or extend a deck pays a change order priced at the actual cost plus the GC's overhead and profit markup, as defined in the contract. This is normal and expected. A change order for a genuine scope addition is not budget drift — it is budget expansion that the owner chose.

Conditions not visible at contract time — subsurface conditions, concealed structural deficiencies in a renovation, utility conflicts not shown on existing drawings — are addressed by the contingency mechanism in the contract. In a well-structured JK fixed-price contract:

  • The subsurface contingency is defined in unit prices, not open-ended cost-plus
  • Concealed conditions that could not have been reasonably anticipated at scope lock are documented with photos and field observations before any remediation work begins
  • The owner is notified within 24 hours of discovery and given a written cost estimate before any additional work proceeds
  • No additional work is performed without the owner's written authorization

The goal of these provisions is transparency without ambiguity. A contingency provision that says "owner will pay for unforeseen conditions at cost-plus" is not a contingency — it is an open-ended liability. A contingency provision that defines the specific conditions, the pricing mechanism, and the authorization process is a protection.

Red Flags in Fixed-Price Custom Home Contracts in Florida

These provisions in a contract you are reviewing should prompt questions or negotiation before signing:

Allowances for items that can be selected now. If a GC is proposing allowances for flooring, plumbing fixtures, and appliances on a project with complete architectural drawings, ask why. These items can be selected. If the GC argues that allowances provide flexibility, that is a sales pitch for a pricing structure that benefits the GC, not you.

"Scope changes at contractor's discretion." Any contract provision that allows the GC to define what constitutes a scope change is a provision that allows the GC to charge change orders for field conditions they should have anticipated.

No defined markup on change orders. If the contract does not specify the overhead and profit markup that will be applied to change orders — 10%, 15%, 20% — then the markup is negotiated under duress when you are mid-project and have no leverage.

No subsurface contingency definition. If the contract has open-ended language about "additional foundation costs at actual cost," ask for a defined unit price schedule tied to the geotechnical report conditions. You can negotiate this before signing. You cannot negotiate it after you discover that the subgrade needs to be excavated 3 feet deeper than anticipated.

No defined response time for owner questions. A GC who does not commit to a written response timeline on owner questions in the contract will not respond any faster once construction starts.

JK Prestige's fixed-price contracts specify the change order markup, the contingency mechanism, the owner question response time (48 hours in writing), and the scope documentation standard. The price in the contract is tied to a specific, attached scope document. If the scope does not change, the price does not change.
