How to Verify a General Contractor's License in Florida Before Signing Anything

May 4, 2026 · 8 min read · JK Prestige Constructor Corp

Before you sign a construction contract in Jacksonville, you can verify your general contractor's (GC's) license, bond, and insurance in under 15 minutes using public databases. Most homeowners skip this step. The ones who skip it and hire unlicensed or underinsured contractors often discover the gap the hard way — after a lien is filed, after an injury on their property, or after a contractor disappears mid-project.

This is not a complicated process. Florida's Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR) publishes license status for every licensed contractor in the state. Here's how to use it, what the results mean, and what to check beyond the license itself.

How to Look Up a Florida GC License on the DBPR Database

Step 1: Go to myfloridalicense.com and click "Verify a License" in the main navigation.

Step 2: Select the license type. For a general contractor, choose "Contractor" from the profession dropdown.

Step 3: Search by the contractor's name, business name, or license number. If the contractor gave you a license number, search by number — it's faster and eliminates name-match ambiguity.

Step 4: Confirm the following fields in the result:

  • License type — should be "Certified General Contractor" (CGC) or "Registered General Contractor" (RGC)
  • Status — must be "Current, Active." Any other status (Delinquent, Null and Void, Suspended) means the license is not valid
  • Name on license — must match the person or entity you're contracting with
  • Expiration date — Florida GC licenses expire every two years; confirm it covers your project timeline

Step 5: Click into the detailed record and check the discipline history tab. Any formal complaints, fines, or license actions are recorded here. One complaint resolved years ago is different from a pattern of recent violations.

Certified vs. Registered: What the License Type Means

Florida has two license tiers for general contractors, and the distinction matters:

A Certified General Contractor (CGC) has passed a state-administered examination and can work anywhere in Florida without additional local approval. The CGC license is issued by the state DBPR.

A Registered General Contractor (RGC) has passed a local county exam and can only work in the jurisdiction where they're registered. An RGC registered in Duval County cannot legally operate as a GC in St. Johns County without separate registration there.

For Jacksonville homeowners, either license is valid for work within Duval County. If your project spans county lines — an addition on a lot split across Duval and St. Johns, for example — confirm the contractor holds appropriate licensure for both jurisdictions.

A licensed general contractor Jacksonville should be able to hand you their CGC or RGC number immediately, without hesitation. If a contractor gives you only a specialty contractor number (roofing, electrical, plumbing), they are not licensed as a GC and cannot legally perform general contracting work on your project.

What Bonding Means and Why It Matters

Bonding is a form of financial guarantee, not insurance. A contractor's license bond (also called a surety bond) is a three-party agreement between the contractor, a surety company, and the state. If the contractor fails to meet their legal obligations — abandons a project, fails to pay subcontractors, violates licensing laws — the bond provides a mechanism for claims up to the bond amount.

Florida requires a $20,000 surety bond for certified general contractors at license application. That number is a minimum floor, not a recommended level of protection for large projects.

For residential projects over $100,000, request a copy of the contractor's bond certificate. Verify the bond is active by calling the surety company listed on the certificate or checking the surety company's online verification portal. A bond document that expired six months ago is not a bond.

Note that bonding does not protect you from poor workmanship directly. It's a backstop against contractor abandonment, unpaid suppliers, and licensing violations — not a quality guarantee.

Insurance: What to Require and How to Verify

This is where most homeowners have the most exposure and the least knowledge. A contractor can be licensed and bonded but carry insurance limits far too low to protect you if something goes wrong on your property.

General Liability (GL) Insurance

General liability insurance covers property damage and bodily injury caused by the contractor's operations. If a crew member damages a neighboring property, drops materials on your car, or causes a fire during construction, the contractor's GL policy responds first.

For residential renovations in Jacksonville, require a minimum of $1M per occurrence and $2M aggregate from any GC you hire. For projects over $500,000, require at least $2M per occurrence.

Ask for a certificate of insurance (COI), not just verbal confirmation. The COI will list the carrier, policy number, coverage limits, and expiration date. Call the carrier's verification line or check the carrier's online portal to confirm the policy is active — certificates can be falsified.

Workers' Compensation Insurance

Workers' compensation is the coverage that protects you if a worker is injured on your property. Without it, an injured worker can sue the property owner directly to recover medical costs and lost wages.

Florida law requires general contractors to carry workers' comp if they have one or more employees. However, sole proprietors can legally exempt themselves. If your GC operates as a one-man operation but sends a crew of five to your house, ask specifically how those workers are covered. If they're independent contractors with no workers' comp, your homeowner's policy is the backstop — and your carrier may deny the claim.

Request the workers' comp COI separately from the GL certificate. Confirm the expiration date and that the policy covers your project address.

Umbrella Coverage

For projects over $250,000, ask whether the contractor carries an umbrella policy — excess liability that kicks in after the primary GL and auto liability limits are exhausted. Umbrella policies are inexpensive relative to the coverage they provide and signal that the contractor has thought carefully about their risk exposure.

Red Flags to Watch Before You Sign

A licensed, bonded, and insured contractor is the baseline. These additional red flags indicate problems regardless of license status:

  • Asks for more than 10% down or $1,000 upfront (whichever is less) — Florida law limits upfront payments to contractors. Requests for 30-50% deposits before work starts are both illegal and a common fraud indicator.
  • No written contract — Any GC who wants to "keep it simple" with a handshake is transferring risk to you.
  • Can't produce insurance certificates within 24 hours — Legitimate contractors have their COIs on file and can send them immediately.
  • No physical address — A P.O. box is not an office. Out-of-state license plates on all vehicles the day of the estimate are worth noting.
  • Unusually low bid — The lowest bid in a competitive field is either leaving something out of scope or planning to make it back in change orders.
  • Post-storm pressure — After hurricanes or major storms, unlicensed contractors flood Jacksonville from other states. Verify license before any post-storm contract.
