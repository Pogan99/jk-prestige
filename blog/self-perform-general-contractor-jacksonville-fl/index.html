Why Jacksonville FL Homeowners Choose a Self-Perform GC Over a Broker-Based Contractor

May 4, 2026 · 7 min read · JK Prestige Constructor Corp

If you've hired a general contractor (GC) in Jacksonville and walked the jobsite to find strangers you've never met doing the work, you experienced a broker-based GC firsthand. The GC you hired called the shots on paper — but the framing, drywall, roofing, and finish carpentry were handed off to whoever was available and willing to work for the margin left after the GC's fee. That model is the industry norm. It doesn't have to be yours.

Self-perform means a GC uses their own employed crews to execute the critical trades — not subcontractors they've never worked with before, not the lowest bidder on a trade packet. At JK Prestige Constructor Corp, our own crews handle framing, concrete, drywall, finish carpentry, and roofing on every project. That distinction is not marketing language. It changes the quality, schedule, and cost of your project in ways that compound from day one.

What a General Contractor in Jacksonville FL Actually Controls

Most GCs in the Jacksonville market operate as coordinators. They write the contract, pull the permits, schedule inspections, and manage the paperwork. The actual construction — the framing that determines whether your walls are plumb, the drywall that determines how your paint looks, the roofing that determines whether you have water damage in five years — gets priced out and awarded to whoever returns a competitive bid.

That's not inherently dishonest. It's just how the brokered model works. The problem is accountability.

When a framing subcontractor cuts corners on blocking, the GC finds out at inspection or after drywall is already hung. At that point the cost to fix it lands on your contract — usually as a change order. The GC manages the repair. The sub who caused the problem is long gone. You pay.

The Accountability Gap in Brokered Construction

A broker-based GC has financial incentive to award trades to the cheapest sub who can start fast. Their margin improves when subcontractor costs go down. Their schedule looks good when trades mobilize quickly, even if that means cycling through multiple subs over the life of the project.

A self-perform GC has the opposite incentive. When your own framing crew is responsible for what they built, they don't cut corners because they'll be the ones fixing it. Quality control stays internal. The superintendent managing drywall is the same person who supervised the framing. Continuity of knowledge across trades is a structural advantage, not a personality trait.

What Trades Self-Perform Matters For Most

Not every trade has the same risk profile. Electrical and plumbing require licensed specialty contractors in Florida regardless of GC model — those will always be specialized subcontractors. The question is which trades sit on the critical path and carry the most quality risk.

Framing determines everything downstream — structural integrity, plumb walls, window and door rough openings, HVAC and MEP (Mechanical, Electrical, and Plumbing) chase locations. Poor framing cannot be corrected cheaply once enclosed. This is the highest-leverage trade to self-perform.

Drywall and finish carpentry determine what you actually see. Tape lines, corner bead, trim reveals — these are the trades most likely to produce punch list surprises when brokered. Our finish carpentry crews handle custom millwork, built-ins, and trim on the same projects where they hung the drywall, which eliminates the finger-pointing that happens when two different subs share a surface.

Roofing carries long-tail risk. A broker-based GC may install your roof with a crew that won't be in business when your shingle warranty needs to be enforced. JK Prestige has a dedicated roofing division — GAF Master Elite certified, CertainTeed Select, Owens Corning — that performs new installations, tear-offs, and warranty work under the same company umbrella.

Concrete matters most on ground-up builds and additions with new footings or slabs. Self-performing concrete gives the GC direct control over mix design, pour timing, and curing — variables that affect structural load ratings and moisture management for the life of the building.

How a Broker-Based Model Generates Change Orders

Change orders are the single most common complaint against GCs in residential construction. The Jacksonville Better Business Bureau files reflect it. Google reviews for every major GC in Duval County reflect it. "The estimate was $X and the final bill was $X plus 30%" is the standard story.

Most change orders in brokered construction aren't caused by genuine scope changes. They're caused by the GC discovering, mid-build, that a subcontractor's bid didn't include something the GC assumed it did, or that a sub walked off the job and the replacement quoted higher, or that material lead times changed after the budget was locked.

When you self-perform, those coordination gaps close. The crew that bids the work is the crew that does the work. There is no margin-compression game between the GC and an arms-length sub. There is no "the electrician damaged the drywall and now we need an extra board" argument between two separate companies.

JK Prestige uses fixed-price contracts. Before we break ground, you have a written scope and a locked price. The JK Way covers exactly how we structure pricing: if the scope doesn't change, the price doesn't change. That commitment is only sustainable because we control the trades that generate the most change-order risk.

What to Ask Any Jacksonville GC Before You Sign

You don't need to take a contractor's word for it when they claim to self-perform. Ask these questions directly:

  1. Which trades are performed by your own employed crews, and which are subcontracted? Get specifics. "We have our own guys" is not an answer. "Our own W-2 framing crew" is.
  2. Who will be on my jobsite from your company every day? A GC who visits once a week and hands off to a sub super is operating as a coordinator.
  3. If a subcontractor you've hired walks off my project, what happens to my contract price? The answer reveals whether you carry that risk or the GC does.
  4. Can I see your OSHA 30 certifications for the supervisors on my project? JK Prestige requires OSHA 30 for all supervisory employees. Many broker-based GCs have no OSHA requirements for subs.
  5. Who is the named person who handles warranty claims after move-in? A brokered GC often has no leverage over a sub who has already moved on.
