Why Jacksonville Homeowners Are Choosing Gut Renovations Over New Construction in 2025

May 4, 2026 · 8 min read · JK Prestige Constructor Corp

---

New construction in Jacksonville runs $350-$500 per square foot in 2025 for a standard custom home — and that range assumes a reasonably straightforward site, no significant foundation work, and normal lead times. At the upper end of that range for a 2,500 square foot home, you are looking at $1.25 million before lot cost, landscaping, and carrying costs during an 18-24 month build.

Meanwhile, a well-located existing home in Riverside, Avondale, or San Marco — with mature trees, established landscaping, walkability to restaurants and shops, and a lot size that could not be acquired new at any price — is trading at $250-$400 per square foot. It needs work. But the math on doing that work is increasingly favorable compared to building from scratch.

This is what is driving gut-reno demand in Jacksonville's mature neighborhoods in 2025. Here is how to think about whether it makes sense for a specific property, what a gut-reno actually costs, and what to look for in a home worth renovating versus one that is not.

---

The Math: Why Gut-Reno Is Often the Better Financial Decision

The comparison that matters

To evaluate gut-reno versus new construction, the comparison is not the purchase price of the existing home. It is the total cost — acquisition plus renovation — against the all-in cost of a new build that achieves a comparable outcome in a comparable location.

A concrete example in Jacksonville's 2025 market:

Option A: New construction

  • Lot in a mature neighborhood (if available): $300,000-$500,000+ — in Riverside and Avondale, lots rarely come to market, and when they do, they are priced accordingly
  • Construction: $400/sqft × 2,600 sqft = $1,040,000
  • Architect and design fees: $80,000-$120,000
  • Landscaping (starting from bare ground): $40,000-$80,000
  • Total: $1.46M-$1.74M

Option B: Gut-reno of an existing home

  • Purchase price: $550,000-$700,000 for a well-located 2,600 sqft home in Riverside or Avondale
  • Gut-reno construction: $150,000-$300,000 depending on scope (full MEP replacement, kitchen, bathrooms, finishes)
  • Architect and design fees: $30,000-$60,000 (less than new build because the structure exists)
  • Total: $730,000-$1,060,000

The bottom end of the gut-reno range is roughly half the cost of new construction. The top end still saves $400,000-$700,000 — and delivers a house in a neighborhood that the new construction cannot replicate.

This math holds up even at a comprehensive gut-reno scope. The things that make new construction expensive — lot preparation, foundation, structural framing, exterior envelope — are already in place in an existing home. You are paying to update the interior and systems, not to build the shell.

What you get that new construction cannot deliver

There are things about a well-located existing home in a mature Jacksonville neighborhood that cannot be bought at any price in new construction:

  • Established trees and landscaping. A 60-year-old live oak in the backyard is not reproducible. Landscaping a new home to comparable maturity takes 15-20 years.
  • Walkability. Riverside and Avondale are among the most walkable neighborhoods in Jacksonville — a Walk Score that no new subdivision can achieve. San Marco is similar.
  • Lot size and character. Historic neighborhood lots are often larger, more irregular, and more private than new subdivision lots, which are optimized for density.
  • School district certainty. School boundaries matter to families. Existing homes in established neighborhoods come with known school assignments. New subdivisions on the suburban fringe sometimes have uncertain or less desirable district placements.

What a Gut-Reno Actually Costs in Jacksonville in 2025

Gut-reno cost depends on scope. Here are the three scope levels JK Prestige sees most frequently:

Cosmetic renovation: $60,000-$120,000

New finishes only — flooring, paint, kitchen and bathroom surface updates (new fixtures, cabinets, countertops without moving walls or rough plumbing), lighting. The home's systems (HVAC, electrical, plumbing) stay. This is not a gut-reno — it's a remodel. It is appropriate when systems are recent and the bones are solid.

Partial gut-reno: $130,000-$250,000

One or two systems replaced (panel upgrade, new HVAC, plumbing from the walls out), kitchen fully reconfigured, primary bathroom rebuilt, minor wall relocation, new flooring throughout. Most of the existing structure stays.

Full gut-reno: $250,000-$400,000+

Everything from studs out. MEP (Mechanical, Electrical, and Plumbing) systems fully replaced, insulation replaced, drywall new throughout, all finishes new, kitchen and bathrooms fully rebuilt, possible structural work (removing load-bearing walls, adding openings). This is the scope that produces a functionally new home inside an existing shell.

What to Look for in a Home Worth Renovating

Not every home in a desirable neighborhood is worth renovating. Here is what to evaluate before you make an offer on a property with renovation intent.

Structural condition

The foundation and primary structural framing determine whether a renovation is financially viable. In NE Florida, the specific things to assess:

  • Foundation type. Older homes in Riverside and Springfield often have concrete block or brick perimeter foundations with piers. These are generally sound but can show settlement or moisture issues. Slab-on-grade is common in mid-century homes and is generally straightforward.
  • Framing condition. Termite history is the primary concern in NE Florida. A home with active or historical termite damage in structural framing members requires assessment before renovation. Treat-and-sister is often feasible; fully compromised primary beams are a budget variable that can change the project economics.
  • Roof structure. Sagging ridgelines or rafters indicate either water damage or inadequate original construction. Assess the attic framing directly, not just the roof surface.

Lot characteristics

  • Size and orientation. Is there room to expand if the program requires it? What is the setback situation for additions?
  • Flood zone status. NE Florida has significant flood zone exposure. A home in the AE flood zone carries mandatory flood insurance requirements and renovation scope restrictions when elevation certificates are involved. FEMA's 50% rule — if renovation costs exceed 50% of the structure's appraised value, the structure must be brought into full current flood code compliance — can change the renovation economics significantly.
  • Drainage. Jacksonville's flat topography creates drainage issues. Assess whether the lot drains properly or has a history of water intrusion.

Mechanical systems age

Replacing all MEP systems in a full gut-reno is expected. But the age and condition of the HVAC, electrical panel, and plumbing affects whether a partial renovation is feasible:

  • An active knob-and-tube or aluminum wiring system requires full electrical replacement before any renovation can be covered and permitted.
  • A panel undersized for modern load requirements (100A or less in a large home) needs replacement.
  • Galvanized plumbing past its service life contributes to a full-replacement recommendation.

When New Construction Wins

Gut-reno is not always the right answer. New construction makes more sense when:

  • The existing home has structural issues that make renovation cost close to new construction value
  • The program requires more square footage than the existing home and lot can support
  • The flood zone or lot limitations make expansion or renovation impractical
  • The buyer's design preferences are specific enough that adapting an existing home is more constrained than building fresh

For buyers who want a custom home in a location where mature-neighborhood lots are available — St. Johns County, Ponte Vedra, Nassau County — new construction may deliver the outcome at comparable total cost because land is more available and less premium-priced.
