Self-Perform Drywall and Framing in a Gut Renovation: How It Changes the Schedule

May 4, 2026 · 7 min read · JK Prestige Constructor Corp

In a typical brokered gut renovation — the kind where the general contractor (GC) coordinates multiple subcontractors but self-performs nothing — framing and drywall are two separate subcontracts. That sounds like a minor detail. In practice, it's where renovations gain two to three weeks of schedule drift without anyone being technically at fault.

When JK Prestige self-performs framing, drywall, and finish carpentry with our own crews, a single superintendent manages the transition between trades. No scheduling gap between subs. No dispute about whose work caused whose defect. No week lost waiting for a drywall sub's calendar to open up.

This post explains exactly how that schedule impact works, what defect responsibility looks like when one GC owns the full sequence, and what it means for a homeowner managing a gut-reno budget.

How a Brokered Gut-Reno Creates Schedule Gaps

Most GCs in Jacksonville and across Florida operate as coordinators. They hold the contract with the homeowner, pull permits, and manage a network of subcontractors who do the actual trade work. For many trades — plumbing, electrical, HVAC — subcontracting is the industry norm and works fine. These are licensed specialty trades with their own inspection gates.

The problem is when framing and drywall are also brokered out.

Here's what happens in practice on a typical brokered gut-reno:

  1. Framing sub finishes rough work. The framing crew completes wall framing, ceiling joists, and any structural modifications. They submit for rough framing inspection.
  1. Rough inspection pass. Inspector approves. This is the trigger for mechanical rough-in (plumbing, electrical, HVAC). Mechanical rough-in runs 2–4 weeks on a typical kitchen/bath scope.
  1. Mechanical rough-ins complete. Now the drywall sub can begin. But the drywall sub wasn't standing by waiting — they have their own project queue. The GC calls them to schedule. The earliest available date is 5–10 business days out.
  1. Drywall begins 1–2 weeks after mechanical rough-in.

That 1–2 week gap is not because the work required it. It happened because the framing crew and the drywall crew are separate businesses with separate schedules, and no one owns the transition except the GC coordinator who has no lever to pull other than a phone call.

On a renovation where the total schedule is 12–16 weeks, a 1–2 week gap between framing completion and drywall start is 6–12% schedule extension — for nothing. No work got done. No inspections occurred. The homeowner was paying carrying costs (rent elsewhere, mortgage on the property being renovated) for an empty week.

What "No Change Orders" Actually Requires

The phrase "no surprise change orders" gets used loosely in renovation marketing. Here's what it actually requires — and why self-perform framing and drywall is part of the answer.

Change orders originate from three sources:

  1. Owner-initiated scope changes: Homeowner decides mid-project to add a window or change a wall location. These are legitimate changes that affect scope and price. A fixed-price contract handles these with a written scope change and a written price before work begins.
  1. Unforeseen conditions: The wall that was supposed to be non-structural turns out to contain a load-bearing member. Hidden rot, old plumbing, asbestos-containing materials. These are genuinely unforeseen and affect scope. The difference between a legitimate unforeseen condition and a manufactured one is documentation — JK photographs and documents what was found before any additional work begins.
  1. Sub-to-sub defect disputes settled with owner change orders: This is the version that shouldn't happen but does in brokered renovations. The framing was done poorly; the drywall sub charges extra to work around it; the GC passes that cost to the owner as an "unforeseen framing condition." This is not an unforeseen condition — it's a consequence of the GC not controlling quality at the framing phase.

When JK self-performs framing and drywall, category three doesn't exist. The quality at each phase is ours to control and ours to fix.

What to Ask Any GC Before Signing a Gut-Reno Contract

Before signing with any GC for a gut renovation, ask these questions specifically about the framing-to-drywall transition:

"Who will perform the framing?" If the answer is "our framing sub," ask who coordinates the drywall schedule and what happens if there's a dispute about framing quality when the drywall crew arrives.

"Who will perform the drywall?" Same line of questioning. If framing and drywall are separate subs, ask the GC how many days the last project lost between framing completion and drywall start.

"What happens if a defect at the framing-drywall transition is found after the framing sub has left the job?" The answer tells you a lot about how the GC handles the gap.

"Is the schedule commitment in the contract?" A fixed-price contract without a schedule commitment is only half-fixed. If the GC can't commit to a project completion date, the schedule risk is entirely yours.
