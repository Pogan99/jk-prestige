Submittal and RFI Log Management: What GC Partners Should Expect From a Self-Perform Crew

June 8, 2026 · 5 min read · JK Prestige Constructor Corp

On most commercial construction projects, submittals and RFIs are managed by the GC and tracked in a master log. Trade subs are expected to submit their required documents on schedule, respond to GC comments, and maintain a clear record of field questions that need design team clarification.

In practice, many framing and drywall subs in Florida treat submittals and RFIs as GC problems. They wait for the GC to chase them for shop drawings. They call the GC foreman when they find a drawing conflict instead of writing a formal RFI. They miss the submittal schedule dates in the subcontract and expect the GC to absorb the delay.

JK Prestige operates differently. Here is what GC partners should require — and what JK delivers as standard.

What Submittals Are Required from a Framing or Drywall Sub

The specific submittal requirements for a trade sub are defined in the project specifications (Division 01 for general requirements and the relevant technical sections for each trade). For framing and drywall, typical required submittals include:

Cold-formed metal framing (Division 05 40 00):

  • Shop drawings showing framing layout, spacing, member sizes, and connection details
  • Product data for stud and track manufacturers
  • Special inspection program (if structural framing requires a special inspector per the IBC)
  • ASTM-certified test reports for structural stud products (required on projects specifying structural framing at 18-gauge or heavier)

Gypsum board assemblies (Division 09 29 00):

  • Product data for gypsum board products, joint treatment, and accessories
  • Fire test reports for fire-rated assemblies (UL or GA designs)
  • Sound test reports for STC-rated assemblies
  • Adhesive and fastener product data if specified

JK submits all required documents in the format specified by the project (PDF submittals to Procore or Submittal Exchange, or paper submittals on projects that require it) within the schedule dates established in the subcontract.

Submittal Schedule: How JK Manages It

At subcontract execution, JK reviews the required submittals against the project master schedule and establishes submittal due dates working backward from the required approval dates. A submittal that must be approved before framing begins needs to be submitted 4 to 6 weeks before framing mobilization — not the week before.

JK maintains its own submittal log with tracking fields for:

  • Submittal description and spec section
  • Date submitted to GC
  • Date returned with GC/design team action
  • Action taken (approved, approved as noted, resubmit)
  • Resubmittal date (if required)
  • Final approval date

The GC receives a copy of JK's submittal log at each weekly report. This means the GC sees the same tracking data JK uses internally — there is no information asymmetry about what has been submitted and what is pending.

How JK Writes RFIs

An RFI (Request for Information) is the formal mechanism for asking the design team a question about the contract documents. RFIs should be written when:

  • The drawings contain a conflict or missing dimension that affects how work is installed
  • A field condition exists that differs from the design assumption
  • The specification allows a substitution request and the sub wants to propose an alternate product

An RFI is not a vehicle for negotiating scope changes. It is a documentation mechanism. JK writes RFIs with:

Clear question: One specific question per RFI. Not "see attached photos for multiple issues" but "At grid C4-C5 on floor 3, the structural drawings show a 3-1/2" stud wall but the MEP drawings show a 5-inch pipe running in the same stud cavity. What is the design intent for stud depth at this location?"

Reference to documents: Drawing sheet number, detail reference, and spec section that applies.

Proposed answer: JK's field recommendation for how to resolve the conflict. The design team can accept the proposed solution, modify it, or reject it — but providing a proposed answer accelerates the response time.

Dates: Date submitted. Required response date (stated explicitly). Late responses that affect the work are noted in the daily report and on the schedule.

What GC Partners Can Expect from JK on the RFI Log

JK does not send RFIs verbally. Every field question that requires a design team response gets a formal written RFI through the GC.

JK does not pile multiple questions into a single RFI. One question, one RFI. This allows the design team to assign each RFI to the right discipline and respond without coordinating multiple sub-questions.

JK tracks open RFI response time. If an RFI is open past the requested response date and the work is on the critical path, JK flags it in writing to the GC with a specific statement: "RFI [number] is 5 days past the requested response date. If a response is not received by [date], the following work will be delayed: [description]. Please confirm status."

What Happens When Submittals Are Late (From Any Party)

Submittal delays create downstream schedule impacts. If JK submits a shop drawing late, the GC's review period is compressed. If the design team returns it late, JK's procurement lead time is compressed. The party who caused the delay owns the schedule impact.

JK maintains a documented record of all submittal submission dates and return dates. If the GC's or design team's review period extended beyond the contract-allowed turnaround time (typically 10 to 14 business days on commercial projects), and that extension affected JK's material procurement or mobilization, JK provides written notice within 48 hours of receiving the late return.

This is not adversarial. It is the documentation discipline that prevents disputes at project closeout.
